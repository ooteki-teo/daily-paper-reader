## Ultrafast electrically controlled magnetism in charge-order-induced ferroelectric altermagnet

The altermagnetism with antiparallel spin alignment exhibits anisotropic spin splitting and may possess an insulating state with a high Neel temperature, while the charge-order-induced ferroelectricity has ultrafast electric polarization switching. Considering that altermagnetism requires breaking space inversion,, the physical foundation for exploring ultrafast electrically controlled magnetism in altermagnetic ferroelectric materials is thus established. In this Letter, based on symmetry analysis and first-principles electronic structure calculations, we predict that LiV 2F6 is a material that simultaneously hosts altermagnetism and charge-order-induced fer-roelectricity. Since both the altermagnetism and ferroelectricity originate from charge order, LiV 2F6 should exhibit strong magnetoelectric coupling. Our calculations indeed demonstrate that electric polarization reversal can induce band spin-polarization switching in LiV 2F6. Moreover, time-dependent density functional theory calculations show that the electric polarization reversal in LiV 2F6 occurs in 15 femtoseconds. Consequently, ultrafast electrically controlled magnetism can be realized in LiV 2F6. Given that LiV 2F6 has already been experimentally synthesized, our work provides a promising material platform for achieving ultrafast electrically controlled magnetism, which might have significant implications for the design of future electronic devices. 

Introduction. Altermagnetism has attracted extensive in-terest due to its combination of the advantages of fer-romagnetic spin polarization and antiferromagnetic zero net magnetization[ 1– 4]. Different from isotropic s-waves spin splitting in ferromagnetism, altermagnetism features anisotropic spin splitting, such as d-wave, g-wave, and i-wave [ 1, 4]. Interestingly, anisotropic spin splitting provides the physical basis for realizing many novel phenomena. Based on anisotropic spin splitting, new physical effects like spin-split spin currents[ 5], piezomagnetic effect[ 6], crystal valley Hall effect[ 7 ], bipolarized Weyl semimetals[ 8 ], and type-II quantum spin Hall effect[ 9 , 10 ] have been theoretically pro-posed, with spin-split spin currents having been experimentally verified[ 11 , 12 ]. Furthermore, due to broken time-reversal sym-metry, various anomalous effects can also emerge in altermag-nets, such as the anomalous Hall effect[ 13 – 17 ], the quantum anomalous Hall effect[ 18 ], and anomalous magneto-optical effects[19, 20]. The combination of altermagnetism with other matter phases represents another promising research direction. For in-stance, the coexistence of altermagnetism and superconductiv-ity may lead to the emergence of nontrivial superconducting states[ 21 , 22 ]; charge density waves can induce a transition from conventional antiferromagnetism to altermagnetism[ 23 ], while altermagnetism itself can also lead to novel anomalous charge density wave phases[ 24 ]. In fact, as the spin moments of magnetic atoms are antiparallel, altermagnetic materials can likely have insulating phases with high Neel temperatures. Therefore, the coexistence of altermagnetism and ferroelec-tricity is physically viable, offering a novel pathway to realize both spin polarization and electric polarization within a sin-gle material. The electric polarization reversal in charge-ordered ferroelec-tric materials stems from electron hopping, resulting in the reversal speed that is significantly faster than that of conven-tional ferroelectric materials. If the charge order can drive a transition from conventional antiferromagnetism or ferromagnetism to an altermagnetic ferroelectric state, the corresponding material would exhibit strong electromagnetic coupling. For upward electric polarization, the spin polarization of the Fermi sur-faces in the valence or conduction band is shown in Fig. 1. When electrons hopping from lower-valence-state magnetic atoms to higher-valence-state magnetic atoms, that is to say, the electric polarization reverses downward, the spin polariza-tion of the Fermi surfaces in the valence or conduction band also reverses (Fig. 1). Consequently, a Type-III multiferroic with ultrafast electrically controlled magnetism can be realized in an altermagnetic ferroelectric material. In this Letter, based on symmetry analysis and first-principles electronic structure calculations, we predict that LiV 2F6 is a material that simultaneously hosts altermagnetism and charge-order-induced ferroelectricity. Since both the al-termagnetism and ferroelectricity originate from charge order, LiV 2F6 exhibits strong magnetoelectric coupling. Furthermore, our calculations demonstrate that electric polarization rever-sal can induce band spin-polarization switching in LiV 2F6.Moreover, time-dependent density functional theory (TDDFT) predicts that the polarization reversal in LiV 2F6 occurs in 15 femtoseconds. Consequently, ultrafast electrically controlled magnetism can be realized in LiV 2F6.

Results and discussion. Based on the principle for achieving ultrafast electrically controlled magnetism in Fig. 1, magnetic atoms of the corresponding material need be fractional va-lence states under high-symmetry structures. This condition favors the emergence of charge order as temperature decreases, subsequently leading to ferroelectricity. We propose that LiV 2F6 might be a candidate material with ultrafast electrically controlled magnetism: the average va-lence state of V atoms is +2.5 and the experimental result of the temperature-dependent magnetic susceptibility of LiV 2F6

clearly exhibits an antiferromagnetic character [ 42 ]. The crys-tal structure of LiV 2F6 was firstly measured in 1983 [ 42 ], as illustrated in Fig. 2(a). It is in a typical trirutile lattice, where Li and V alternatively occupy 1/3 and 2/3 MF 6 octahedra along 

z axis. The experiment gives that all V atoms are equivalent and its space group is P 42/mnm , with the corresponding point group D 4h, which includes generators C 4z (1/2, 1/2, 1/2), C2x(1/2, 1/2, 1/2), and I. Since LiV 2F6 is a nonsymmorphic space group, its unit cell contains four V atoms (Fig. 2(a)). In this paper, we name this structure the high-symmetry structure (HSS). Based on the results calculated above, for subsequent studies on the ferroelectric and ultrafast electrically controlled mag-netic properties of LiV 2F6, we choose an on-site Coulomb interaction U of 5 eV for d orbits of V atoms. In LSS LiV 2F6,alternatively arranging V 2+ and V 3+ ions induce ferroelec-tricity, which is similar to LiFe 2F6 [ 25 , 30 , 43 ]. Then, we calculate the ferroelectric polarization P with the Berry phase method [ 37 , 38 ], giving P = 12 .1 μC/cm 2 along the z-axis for the altermagnetic AM2 state. This is faithfully consistent with the result of classical point charge model (10.6 μC/cm 2). Moreover, we also use the climbing image nudged elastic band (CI-NEB) method[ 39 ] to simulate the process of switching its ferroelectric polarization, as shown in Fig.3 (a). The en-ergy barrier is about 0.55 eV/f.u., larger than that of LiFe 2F6

[ 25 , 30 ] and comparable to that of BiFeO 3 [ 44 ]. Notably, in our CI-NEB calculation, the magnetic structure of LiV 2F6

is fixed as AM2, although FM is the ground state magnetic structure in HSS LiV 2F6. Therefore, LiV 2F6 indeed exhibits charge-order-induced ferroelectricity. More importantly, the charge-order-induced ferroelectricity also causes LiV 2F6 to transition from FM state to an alter-magnetic AM2 state, indicating that LiV 2F6 exhibits strong electromagnetic coupling. Therefore, electrically controlled magnetism may be achievable in LiV 2F6. Then, we calculated the band structure of LiV 2F6 with downward electric polarization, as shown in Fig.4 (c). Comparing Fig.4 (b) and Fig.4 (c), the band structure features of LiV 2F6 with upward and downward electric polarization are identical, but the spin polarization of the bands is reversed. This indicates that electric control of magnetism can indeed be achieved in LiV 2F6. On the other hand, the electric polarization rever-sal in charge-order-induced ferroelectricity is realized through electrons hopping, which implies that electric polarization switching in LiV 2F6 is significantly faster than in conventional ferroelectric materials. Then, we simulate the photoinduced ultrafast charge transfer dynamics in LiV 2F6 under laser excitation. Figs.4(d) and (e) display the time evolution of the charge on the four distinct vanadium sites, which exhibit a clear two-stage dynamics. For the spin-up channel (Fig.4(d)), the charge on the V1 site (for-mal valence +2 ) decays rapidly from 10.56 e to 10.43 e within 15 fs, and then oscillates around 10.43 e in the 15–25 fs time window. Concurrently, the charge on the V4 site (valence +3 )shows a complementary increase from 10.37 e to 10.54 e. This anti-phase behavior constitutes a direct charge transfer from V1 to V4. An identical and synchronized charge transfer process is observed for the spin-down channel from V3 to V2 (Fig.4(e)). The combined dynamics in both spin channels demonstrate a net displacement of charge between the different valence states, as schematically illustrated in Figs.4(b-c). Critically, this laser-driven charge redistribution directly reverses the local electric dipoles, leading to a coherent flipping of the macroscopic fer-roelectric polarization on the ultrafast timescale. Therefore, LiV 2F6 is indeed a type-III altermagnetic ferroelectric material that exhibits ultrafast electrically controlled magnetism. According to the study of LiV 2F6, altermagnetic materials exhibiting ultrafast electrically controlled magnetism need pos-sess two key features: 1) In the high-symmetry structure, the magnetic atoms are in fractional valence states, which facili-tates the emergence of charge ordering at low temperatures; 2) The inversion symmetry breaking induced by charge ordering can simultaneously lead to ferroelectricity and altermagnetism, but the material in its HSS must not already be in an alter-magnetic state. For example, LiFe 2F6 exhibits altermagnetism both before and after the appearance of charge ordering, yet it does not demonstrate ultrafast electrically controlled mag-netism. These two features provide theoretical guidance for the further search for more altermagnetic multiferroic materials that exhibit ultrafast electrically controlled magnetism. Based on symmetry analysis, first-principles electronic struc-ture calculations, and time-dependent density functional theory, we predict that LiV 2F6 is an altermagnetic multiferroic mate-rial exhibiting ultrafast electrically controlled magnetism. Ac-cording to our calculations and analysis, ultrafast electrically controlled magnetism originates from the simultaneous emer-gence of ferroelectricity and altermagnetism induced by charge order. Furthermore, we summarize the necessary conditions for discovering materials possessing ultrafast electrically con-trolled magnetism. Considering that LiV 2F6 has already been experimentally synthesized, our work not only provides an ex-cellent material platform for investigating ultrafast electrically controlled magnetism but also offers guidance for discovering additional materials exhibiting ultrafast electrically controlled magnetism.
