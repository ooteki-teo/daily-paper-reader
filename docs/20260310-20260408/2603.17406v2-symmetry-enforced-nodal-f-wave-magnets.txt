## Symmetry-Enforced Nodal f -Wave Magnets 

Moritz M. Hirschmann, 1, ∗ Akira Furusaki, 1 and Max Hirschberger 1, 2, †

> 1

RIKEN Center for Emergent Matter Science (CEMS), Wako, Saitama 351-0198, Japan

Department of Applied Physics, The University of Tokyo, Bunkyo, Tokyo 113-8656, Japan 

(Dated: March 20, 2026) Owing to their relevance for spintronics, electronic band splitting and spin-polarization textures in magnets are active areas of research. In non-collinear magnets, alternating spin textures can arise both for isolated bands and for intersecting band pairs with nodal splitting. This raises the question of whether p, f, . . . -wave magnets should be defined by their spin polarization or their band splitting. To resolve this ambiguity, we introduce spin-space symmetries that couple the spin polarization and splitting textures for all bands. Focusing on the nodal f -wave magnet, we construct a tight-binding model of itinerant electrons on a honeycomb bilayer coupled to a non-collinear magnetic texture. Analytic expressions for spin polarization and splitting reveal the dependence on hopping and exchange coupling. We predict a canting-induced spin conductivity arising from the nodal structure of the splitting. Furthermore, the f -wave magnet in the bulk can induce p-wave magnetism on the surface. This surface p-wave character leads to a bulk-forbidden Edelstein effect with f -wave anisotropy. 

Introduction – Magnetic textures can induce an energy splitting of itinerant electrons. Even in ferromagnets, this splitting is inhomogeneous [1, 2] because spin-orbit coupling (SOC) and orbital polarization vary across the Brillouin zone. Likewise, a spatially inhomogeneous mag-netic texture may produce an anisotropic splitting and polarization textures in momentum space [3–5]. Momentum-dependent spin splitting can be classified by the presence of gapless points, lines, or planes. For ex-ample, collinear altermagnets at negligible SOC exhibit an anisotropic spin splitting that vanishes in nodal lines in two dimensions (2D) or planes in three dimensions (3D) [6–8]. A collinear magnetic texture implies that the spin sz along the collinear order is a good quantum number at every momentum and determines the split-ting structure. For example, in a d-wave altermagnet, a spin-up band (red) can be mapped to a spin-down band (blue) by a fourfold rotation, leading to lines of vanishing splitting, ∆ s(k) = 0, see Fig. 1(a). To describe the relation between spin polarization and band splitting, one may define a splitting |∆s,n (k)| ≡ 

En+1 (k) − En(k) between energies of bands n + 1 and 

n that would be degenerate without magnetic exchange. The spin polarization defines the sign of ∆ s,n (k). For altermagnets, we define ∆ s,n (k) > 0 if the spin sz

expectation value of band n is negative, sz,n (k) ≡⟨ψn(k)|sz |ψn(k)⟩ < 0. The spin splitting ∆ s,n (k) can be expanded in real spherical harmonics labeled by l ∈ N0.Ferromagnets correspond to s-wave ( l = 0) and collinear altermagnets exhibit d, g, i, . . . -wave ( l even) [8]. The pa-rameter l corresponds to the number of nodal lines in 2D (dashed lines in Fig. 1(a)) or of nodal planes in 3D. To realize odd-parity magnets ( p, f, . . . ), two types of systems have been considered: (I) Collinear magnets with broken spinless time-reversal symmetry by Floquet driv-ing [9–11] or orbital magnetism [12–14]; and (II) non-collinear magnets [15]. For magnets (II), unlike altermag-nets, spin is not a good quantum number. Instead, their spin polarization varies with momentum even at non-zero splitting ∆ s,n (k). For example, the spin polarization tex-ture of a non-collinear magnet, Fig. 1(d), shows a con-tinuous change from Γ to M points. Nevertheless, non-collinear systems are used in experimental realizations of 

p-wave magnets [16, 17] and theoretical studies of odd-parity magnets [15, 18–26]. Specifically, the splitting in p-wave magnets leads to anisotropic transport [15, 16], and if time-reversal symmetry is broken there is an anoma-lous Hall effect arising from the gapped nodes [16, 21]. Likewise, the p-wave Edelstein effect requires both spin polarization and band splitting [18, 27]. How can we integrate non-collinear magnets into the 

s, p, d, . . . -wave classification scheme shown in Fig. 1(a), where ∆ s(k) describes spin polarization and splitting? To answer this question, we focus on f -wave magnets, as they have so far mostly been studied in collinear magnets with fully polarized bands [9, 10, 12, 28– 31]. Since the previously discussed non-collinear models [19, 20, 22, 25, 26] do not exhibit an f -wave splitting for all bands, we present symmetry arguments that en-force f -wave splitting and polarization. This symmetry-enforced low-energy description implies a characteristic bulk spin conductivity, if the order is canted, and an anisotropic surface Edelstein effect.

Tight-binding model – We construct a minimal 2D Hamiltonian H(k) for electrons in an f -wave magnet that is symmetric under eCs, eT , C3z , and My , see Fig. 1(b). The nearest-neighbor hopping t1 stays within each hon-eycomb layer and a long-range hopping t2 joins adjacent layers. If t1 = 0 or t2 = 0, the f -wave splitting van-ishes in our model. The magnetic unit cell comprises 4 sites, each with an s-d coupling J between the electron and a local magnetic moment. Once one of the copla-nar moments is chosen, symmetries eCs and My fix the remaining moments. To avoid a collinear magnetic tex-ture, the chosen moment may not be along the x or y

axis; we choose the moment at r1 along ( −1, 1, 0). This model can be extended to 3D, see End Matter. 3The band structure, Fig. 1(c), exhibits the expected splitting with nodal lines along Γ-K and K-M, as well as Dirac points at K inherited from the honeycomb lattice [39]. In the radial direction of Fig. 1(d), a sign change in the spin polarization appears at the avoided crossing at energy E = ±3.5. Sign changes of polarization without a band degeneracy are common in odd-parity magnets [15, 19, 33]. While the splitting ∆ s(k) has f -wave char-acter for small k in Fig. 1(e), the sign change in spin polarization leads to a sign change of ∆ s(k) in the radial direction.

f -wave symmetry constrained Edelstein effect – The Edelstein effect refers to a non-zero net spin ( δs )i caused by an applied electric field Ej . Symmetry constraints on the Edelstein tensor χij , defined as ( δs )i = χij Ej , follow from Neumann’s principle: Symmetry transformations leave the tensor invariant [42, 43]. For p and f -wave magnets, the spin rotation eCs removes the rows of two spin components, χxj = χyj = 0 for any j ∈ { x, y, z }.4Non-vanishing χzj components were calculated for p-wave magnets [18, 27]. For f -wave magnets, the three-fold rotation C3z conserves the remaining spin compo-nent but rotates the spatial components, setting χzj = 0 for j = x, y , leaving only χzz . Discussion – Since the surface Edelstein effect is a con-sequence of symmetry reduction, it can be generalized to 

h- and j-wave magnets. Magneto-optical spectroscopy [46, 47], or spin-sensitive transport measurements [48– 51] may detect such a spin accumulation. In quasi-2D systems, an f -wave magnet can be realized even with SOC. A spinful mirror symmetry Mz sets the polarizations sx,n (k) = sy,n (k) = 0 at all momenta. For example, the magnetic layer group P ¯6m2 (or Pc¯6m2 with time reversal) leads to an f -wave magnet. While a non-collinear magnetic texture with this symmetry requires at least a sixfold Wyckoff position, with SOC, already Bernal bilayer graphene can exhibit f -wave spin polar-ization [52]. We have shown that symmetry-enforced p, f, . . . -wave splitting in non-collinear magnets matches the spherical harmonic classification of altermagnets [7, 30] (Fig. 1(a)). With linear spin conductivity and p-wave splitting on the edges, we identified characteristic properties that arise 5from nodal f -wave magnets. The surface Edelstein effect, on the other hand, is also symmetry-allowed for f -wave polarization sz,n (k) ∝ ky (3 k2

Dirac points and edge states – The symmetries, C3z

and spinful My , protect Dirac points at K and K’ with 

π-Berry phase, which are equivalent to the Dirac points in graphene [39]. Here, the group structure related to C3z

and My is identical with and without spin, thus the Dirac points persist in the magnetic structure. While such Dirac points also arise in the f -wave model of Ref. [19], they are not generally enforced in f -wave magnets [9, 10]. For the x termination, i.e, the armchair termination, there are no edge states as K and K’ are mapped onto the same momentum. In contrast to graphene, eCs pro-tects with open boundary conditions two linear crossings (Fig. 3(c)). These crossings exhibit p-wave splitting at 

E = ±3.4 corresponding to a local maximum in Fig. 3(d). The Dirac points are well separated in energy from the dominant contribution to the f -wave Edelstein effect and are therefore not essential for it.
