# Nanoscale electronic variations in altermagnetic α-MnTe

(Dated: March 17, 2026) Altermagnets exhibit spin-split electronic bands like ferromagnets, yet they are magnetically com-pensated like collinear antiferromagnets. Altermagnets thus combine the benefits of ferromagnets and antiferromagnets, opening routes to tailored materials and applications. However, reported bulk signatures such as the anomalous Hall response in candidate altermagnets have been inconsistent across samples, suggesting that inhomogeneity may affect their functionality in electronic devices. Here, we use low-temperature scanning tunneling microscopy and spectroscopy to map the local electronic landscape of α-MnTe on atomically flat cleaved single crystals. We resolve two distinct electronic regions. In Region A, the chemical potential lies near the valence-band edge and varies by 

∼100 meV on the nanometer length scale. In Region B, the chemical potential lies near the middle of a wider band gap. We further identify an incommensurate charge modulation with periodicity (2.5 ±0.1) a, observed exclusively in Region A. Our work establishes that α-MnTe can exhibit signifi-cant electronic non-uniformity, suggesting that nanoscale characterization is essential for its reliable use in electronic applications. 

Antiferromagnetic materials are promising candidates for next-generation memory devices due to their ultrafast switching, which can operate in the terahertz range, far surpassing the gigahertz rates of ferromagnets [1]. Unlike ferromagnetic materials, however, conventional antifer-romagnets rely on weak relativistic spin-orbit coupling to achieve the spin-dependent transport that is critical for electronic readout of the magnetic state [2]. Re-cently, a new classification of magnetic materials, known as altermagnets, has emerged as a promising platform for novel electronic devices. Altermagnets exhibit an-tiferromagnetic ordering along with strongly spin-split electronic bands, comparable to those of ferromagnets [3, 4]. This unique combination of properties offers the best of both worlds, potentially combining the ultra-fast switching speeds of antiferromagnets with the strong spin-polarization of ferromagnets. While initial evidence for altermagnetism emerged from anomalous Hall measurements in leading candi-date materials such as RuO 2 [5], Mn 5Si 3 [6], and α-MnTe [7], subsequent experiments yielded mixed re-sults. The anomalous Hall effect in Mn 5Si 3 showed pro-nounced sample-to-sample variation linked to impurity differences [8, 9]. Furthermore, spin-split bands were not detected in RuO 2 [10], indicating strong sensitiv-ity to sample-dependent factors such as defects [11] and strain [12]. In contrast to RuO 2, altermagnetic spin split-ting has been observed in α-MnTe [13], yet variations in strain, conductivity, and the N´ eel vector direction cause significant variations of the anomalous Hall effect [14, 15]. Though recent work imaged monolayer and bilayer MnTe [16–18], these films exhibit a weakly-buckled honeycomb structure with in-plane lat-tice constants ranging from a = 4 .3–4 .6 ˚ A, deviating sig-nificantly from bulk α-MnTe. Crucially, the ultrathin film structure is symmetry-incompatible with altermag-netism [18]. Therefore, high-resolution imaging on pris-tine bulk α-MnTe is imperative to resolve electronic dis-crepancies relevant to altermagnetic devices. Here, we use scanning tunneling microscopy and spec-troscopy (STM/S) to map electronic disorder on the bulk altermagnetic semiconductor α-MnTe. Spectroscopy re-veals two distinct electronic environments. In Region A, the chemical potential lies near the valence band and varies continuously by ∼100 meV over nanometer length scales. In Region B, the chemical potential is positioned near the center of a wider band gap. We also discover a charge modulation with an incommensurate periodicity of (2 .5±0.1) a, where a is the lattice constant. We observe this modulation only in Region A and only at positive sample biases. Our results show that as-grown α-MnTe is electronically non-uniform, and bulk signatures should be interpreted in this context. Bulk α-MnTe crystallizes in the NiAs-type hexagonal structure (space group P 63/mmc ). Below the N´ eel tem-perature ( TN ≈ 307 K in bulk [20]), α-MnTe orders in an A-type antiferromagnetic ground state where the two Mn sublattices compensate each other, and are connected by a combination of a two-fold spin rotation and a real-space six-fold screw-axis rotation [ C2|| C6t1/2] (Fig.1(a)) [21, 22]. We grow α-MnTe single crystals from Te flux, and confirm the single phase of our samples by powder X-ray diffraction [19]. The Fourier transform (FT) of the topograph, shown in Fig. 1(d), displays six Bragg peaks consistent with the hexagonal structure of α-MnTe. In addition to the atomic corrugation in Fig. 1(c), we observe a continuous long-range variation in the topog-raphy. To distinguish between structural and electronic sources of this variation, we acquire topographs at differ-ent sample biases (Fig. 2(a,b)). We observe that the con-trast of the long-range background is inverted between positive- and negative-bias topographs. To focus on the long-range variation, we apply a low-pass filter to the to-pographs. The cross-correlation of these filtered images (Fig. 2(f)) demonstrates anti-correlation with maximum coefficient α ∼ − 0.54. Therefore, the dominant long-range contrast is strongly bias dependent, indicating it primarily reflects electronic variations. To uncover the electronic origin of the long-range vari-ation in Fig. 2(a,b), we measure a grid of 64 × 64 dif-ferential conductance ( dI/dV ) spectra covering the same field of view. The dI/dV is proportional to the local den-sity of states (LDOS) as a function of energy. Fig. 2(c) shows a spatial map of dI/dV at sample bias −0.5 V near the top of the valence band. We then sort all dI/dV 

spectra according to their intensity at −0.5 V into ten bins, and present the averaged spectrum for each bin in (Fig. 2(d)). All spectra exhibit an asymmetric gap of ap-proximately 1 eV, with the chemical potential closer to the valence-band edge. This asymmetry is expected be-cause as-grown α-MnTe is slightly hole-doped [7, 23, 24] due to native vacancies that shift the chemical potential towards the valence band [25]. On the nanoscale, the chemical potential varies by ∼100 meV. This variation naturally explains the observed topographic contrast in-version: a local upward shift of the chemical potential in-creases the LDOS at positive sample biases and decreases it at negative biases. We show that the shift of the chemi-cal potential is spatially correlated with the nanoscale to-pographic variations in Fig. 2(f). We also observe in-gap states that appear stronger when the chemical potential is closer to the valence band (Fig. 2(e)). The positive bias topograph shown in Fig. 2(b) ex-hibits a periodic charge modulation, which is absent at the negative bias (Fig. 2(a)). Fig. 3(a,b) show the FT of these topographs, where six broad peaks along the Bragg peak directions appear in the positive-bias FT, but are absent in the negative-bias FT. To quantify this contrast, we take linecuts of both FT maps through one Bragg-peak direction (Fig. 3(c)), where a peak appears at the wave-vector QCM only for Vs = +1 .5 V. The ex-tracted QCM corresponds to a real-space periodicity of (2 .5 ± 0.1) a, indicative of an incommensurate modula-tion. By applying a high-pass filter to the topograph, we isolate the real-space pattern of the charge modulation (Fig. 3(d)). The modulation is short-ranged and spa-tially disordered, reminiscent of disordered charge den-sity wave systems [26]. To quantify the coherence length of these modulations, we calculate a 2D autocorrelation map of the filtered topograph (Fig. 3(e)) and show that the coefficient decays to nearly zero after roughly 1.5 nm (Fig. 3(f)). In different areas of the sample, we observe re-gions with qualitatively distinct electronic properties (Fig. 4(a)). We assign Region A to areas that ex-hibit a periodic charge modulation at positive bias and chemical potential located near the valence-band edge (Fig. 4(b,d)). In contrast, Region B shows no peri-odic charge modulation and displays a wider gap with the chemical potential positioned near the gap center (Fig. 4(c,e)). Although Region B exhibits long-range to-pographic variation (Fig. 4(c)), it does not exhibit the contrast inversion between positive and negative biases characteristic of Region A (Fig. 2). Beyond the pure areas of Regions A and B, we identify fields of view in which both electronic behaviors coex-ist. In these mixed areas, the surface exhibits an abrupt nanoscale boundary separating A–like and B–like envi-ronments (Fig. 4(f)). Mapping this bimodal contrast spatially (Fig. 4(h)) reveals the sharp nanoscale separa-tion between the two electronic environments: Region A, characterized by a ∼1 eV gap with the chemical poten-tial near the valence band, and Region B, characterized by a ∼1.5 eV gap with the chemical potential near the center of the gap (Fig. 4(k)). In Fig. 4(i), we confirm that the periodic charge modulation appears exclusively in Region A fragments, by masking the positive-bias to-pograph of Fig. 4(f) and calculating the FT of A and B fragments separately, shown on the right and left, respec-tively. Importantly, we confirm continuous atomic resolu-tion across these abrupt electronic changes, by mapping the negative-bias topograph in the same view of view (Fig. 4(g)). Similar to pure A regions, we observe no charge modulation at negative sample bias throughout this field of view. We reveal that α-MnTe exhibits two distinct types of disorder: a continuous, nanometer-scale variation of the chemical potential, and an abrupt spatial segregation be-tween two distinct electronic environments (labeled Re-gion A and B in Fig. 4). Within Region A, the chemical potential skews towards the valence band and varies by 

∼100 meV over several nanometers (Fig. 2(c,d)). This variation illustrates that as-grown α-MnTe cannot be as-sumed to have a spatially-uniform electronic structure, and that bulk signatures should be interpreted with a 

∼100 meV broadening of the chemical potential in mind. Doping inhomogeneity is a likely origin of this disor-der: since as-grown α-MnTe is slightly hole-doped due to native vacancies [25], a spatial inhomogeneity of va-cancy concentrations would produce local chemical po-tential shifts, consistent with our observations. The ∼1 eV gap regions (Region A) are abruptly segre-gated in space from the ∼1.5 eV gap regions (Region B), where the chemical potential lies approximately at mid-gap (Fig. 4(e,k)). This electronic segregation is further reflected in the presence of the periodic ∼ 2.5a charge modulation only in Region A (Figs. 2(b) and 4(b,f)). The correlation between the spectroscopic signatures and the charge modulation indicates that proximity of the valence band to the Fermi level is necessary for the formation of the charge modulation. Moreover, the absence of the modulation at negative sample bias (Figs. 2(a) and 4(g)) argues against a structural surface reconstruction within Region A and instead supports an electronic origin for this charge modulation. In conclusion, we demonstrate that as-grown α-MnTe exhibits two electronically distinct regions. In Region A, the chemical potential lies near the valence band and varies by ∼100 meV over nanometer length scales. In Re-gion B, the band gap is larger and the chemical potential lies closer to mid-gap. We further discover an incommen-surate charge modulation with a period of ∼ 2.5a that ap-pears only in Region A and only at positive sample bias. Our results demonstrate that a detailed understanding of the nanoscale electronic landscape of α-MnTe is essential for its implementation in future electronic devices. 55 nm 5 nm 5 nm
