Altermagnetism, a recently identified magnetic phase that combines vanishing net magnetization with momentum-dependent spin splitting, challenges the conventional dichotomy between ferromagnets and antifer-romagnets. While several candidate materials have been proposed, direct experimental evidence linking crystal symmetry, electronic structure and d-wave spin polarization remains scarce. Here we report the visualiza-tion of a metallic d-wave altermagnet in KV 2Se 2O. Through spin-selective scanning tunneling microscopy powered by a topological insulator tip, we uncover symmetry-protected momentum-dependent spin splitting that follows a characteristic d-wave form factor. Our results establish KV 2Se 2Oas a tunable platform to study the interplay between spin–valley locking, Fermi-surface instability and unconventional magnetism, and open a path-

The discovery of non-relativistic spin-split collinear altermagnets [1–18] has marked a turning point in the study of unconventional magnetism [19–26]. Unlike conventional N´ eel antiferromagnetism (AFM), altermagnets (AM) combine compensated magnetic sublattices with momentum-dependent spin splitting, offering a pathway to electrically generate and ma-nipulate spin currents without stray fields or net magnetization [27–32]. This new paradigm has rapidly advanced from theoretical prediction to experimental realization in materials such as the van der Waals AV2Se 2O and AV2Te 2O family ( A = Alkali metals) [33–40], iden-tified as a d-wave altermagnet in its monolayer [4] and a hidden altermagnet in the bulk [39]. Within this material family, the Lieb lattice geometry of the V 2O plane gives rise to spin-polarized Fermi surfaces with quasi-1D flat segments, manifesting as a distinctive electronic structure that enables nearly 100% efficiency in charge-to-spin current conversion [41, 42]. The band structure of KV 2Se 2O has been investigated by angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy (ARPES) [33]. Yet, spin-resolved ARPES measurements are limited by mag-netic domains and the inherently low efficiency of spin detection [43, 44]. Until now, a smok-ing gun evidence of AM spin splitting on the microscopic scale is highly desired. Complemen-tary real-space techniques capable of visualizing momentum-dependent spin polarization at atomic resolution remain challenging. Moreover, altermagnetism inherently evades conven-tional magnetic probes: its compensated order yields no net magnetization, while standard spin-polarized tunneling methods via ferromagnetic tips can generate stray fields that lo-cally perturb the delicate spin arrangement, potentially affecting the measurement [45, 46]. Therefore, a probe that minimizes such perturbations while maintaining the sensitivity to spin polarization is essential for reliably resolving the intrinsic spin structure. Topological Kondo insulators offer a promising solution to this challenge. In SmB 6,strong correlations drive band inversion and produce Dirac surface states with helical spin–momentum locking [47]. When fabricated as a nanowire scanning tunneling microscopy (STM) tip, SmB 6 acts as an intrinsic directional spin filter [48, 49]: the tunneling current is spin-polarized and the spin orientation could be flipped by bias voltage, rather than by an external magnetic moment [46]. In this work, we use SmB 6 nanowire–based STM probes to investigate the surface electronic structure of the AM material KV 2Se 2O. Using this topological tunneling geometry, we directly observe a clear band splitting on the KV 2Se 2O3surface in both real space and momentum space, consistent with symmetry-protected AM spin polarization. The observed splitting is robust, demonstrating a powerful and minimally invasive route to accessing AM band structures. KV 2Se 2O was synthesized using KSe as the self-flux agent. The crystal structure is shown in Fig. 1A, which has a tetragonal structure with a space group P 4/mmm . The structure consists of V 2O, Se, and K layers stacked sequentially along the c-axis. Specifically, the V 2O plane realizes a Lieb lattice geometry, in which the two symmetry-inequivalent V atoms within each unit cell are related by a four-fold rotation about the c-axis ( C4z ) or by a mirror reflection through the [1¯ 10] plane ( M1¯ 10 ). In the AM phase, these two V sites carry opposite local moments, and the magnetic structure remains invariant under the spin-space symmetries [ Us|| C4z ] and [ Us|| M1¯ 10 ], where Us denotes a global spin flip. These symmetries impose the following constraints on the band structure: E↑(kx, k y) = E↓(−ky, k x)and E↑(kx, k y) = E↓(ky, k x). Spin degeneracy is therefore lifted at arbitrary momentum and is enforced only along the symmetry-protected diagonal lines kx = ±ky. This symmetry characterization identifies the monolayer KV 2Se 2O as a d-wave AM [4, 33]. Figure 1B provides a top-down view (projection) of the K and V 2O planes. The local magnetic moments on each V site are oriented along the c-axis and are distinguished by red and blue colors, indicating an in-plane antiferromagnetic order. Sample cleaves in the K plane and leaves almost half of the K atoms on the terminated surface. The residual K atoms undergo surface reconstruction, forming a √2a × √2a lattice, as presented in Fig. 1C. Below N´ eel temperature TN , a second phase transition occurs around T ′

We therefore focus on sample regions containing native atomic defects, which act as effective scattering centers. In typical topographic images of KV 2Se 2O, the most common defects appear as paired protrusions. Upon closer examination, these protrusions form arrow-like structures with four orthogonal orientations, as indicated by the white-dashed arrows in Fig. 1D. These arrow-like features reflect the underlying anisotropy of the electronic structure of the V2O plane, with each orientation corresponding to preferential scattering along directions dictated by the nearly-flat Fermi surface segments. At low temperature, we measured the dI/d V spectra using both a normal W-tip and a topological SmB 6-tip. The W-tip provides 5access to the total local density of states (LDOS), while the SmB 6-tip enables an novel spin-sensitive tunneling process [48] (see Fig. S8 and S9 for tip fabrication and calibration). Both dI/d V spectra demonstrate similar SDW gap features within a bias range of −15 to +30 meV (see Fig. 1E). The LDOS inside the gap is very flat, indicating high sample quality. The agreement between the two tips confirms that both measurements reflect the intrinsic electronic properties of KV 2Se 2O. From the STS spectra, we extracted an SDW gap size of approximately 40 meV. The slightly smaller gap observed with the SmB 6-nanowire tip, due to additional LDOS around 10 meV, is due to the Fano resonance peak of SmB 6.We next measure the electronic band structure of KV 2Se 2O experimentally and compare it with the theoretical prediction of AM spin-splitting. To achieve this, we employ the Fourier transform of quasi-particle interference (QPI) patterns acquired with a W tip, which offers high momentum-space resolution. The defects generate pronounced Friedel oscillations with a cross-shaped pattern, which extend several nanometers along the x and y directions. A fast Fourier transform (FFT) of Fig. 2A reveals the QPI patterns associated with multiple scattering wavevectors (see Fig. 2B). By further optimizing the FFT contrast, an additional pair of peaks can also be resolved, indicative of a coexisting long-range one-dimensional electronic stripe order (Fig. S10). Figure 2C shows the band struc-ture near the Fermi level, calculated from a minimal model that incorporates both AM and SDW orders. The resulting constant energy contours (CEC) at 40 meV (inset of Fig. 2C) comprises four nearly parallel bands along the kx ≈ ± π/ 2a and ky ≈ ± π/ 2a directions. These orthogonal Fermi-surface segments retain opposite spin polarization and satisfy the symmetry [ Us|| M¯110 ] for the spin configuration shown in Fig. 1C, confirming that AM char-acter persists even in the presence of SDW order. Because the SDW gap ( ∼ 40 meV) is substantially smaller than the AM spin-splitting ( ∼1.8 eV), the system predominantly maintains its d-wave AM character. Before doing so, we verify that the presence of impurities does not perturb the underlying electronic orders. To this end, we acquire d I/d V line cuts across a paired impurity (see Figs. 3A and B). The results indicate that the SDW gap itself is largely unaffected by the impurity, as is the AM order. Consequently, our analysis of impurity-driven QPI patterns can reliably reflect the underlying AM band structure. However, a small peak appears 8around Vb = −3 mV, signaling the presence of in-gap bound states trapped by the lattice defect—although these states are not the focus of the present study. Now, we examine the impurity-induced standing waves along the x and y directions using both W- and SmB 6-tips (see Fig. 3C and D), at bias voltages of Vb = 60 mV and 70 mV, respectively. While both tips reveal standing waves, a striking difference emerges in the relative amplitude and phase of the oscillations along the two orthogonal directions. Measurements with the W tip show nearly identical LDOS oscillations along ±x and ±y

(blue and red dashed lines in Fig. 3C), with the same period and phase when plotted with the impurity as the origin (see line profiles in Fig. 3E). In stark contrast, measurements with the SmB 6-tip reveal a strong anisotropy: the LDOS is enhanced along ±x but suppressed along ±y (Fig. 3D). This asymmetry becomes evident when comparing the standing waves extracted along the dashed lines in Fig. 3D. This anisotropic electronic response is a global feature, as corroborated by large-field-of-view dI/dV maps and their corresponding Fourier transforms across extended positive and negative biases (e.g., ±65 mV and ±70 mV; see Fig. S11). Zooming into the individual impurities, the corresponding line profiles, shown in Fig. 3F, further reveal clear oscillations with a π phase shift between x and y directions. Figures 3G and H schematically illustrate a possible origin of this phase shift: the spin-sensitive SmB 6-tip experiences enhanced or suppressed tunneling current depending on both the spin-selective tunneling process and the spin conserved scattering within the spin-split 

dxz and dyz orbitals. In contrast, the spin-insensitive W-tip visualizes only the total LDOS, which remains isotropic. This asymmetric response is reminiscent of spin-polarized stipes observed in Fe(Se,Te) using SmB 6-nanowire tips [48]. In that system, the measured LDOS oscillates between spin-up and spin-down atomic sites (real space). Moreover, these oscillations reverse phase upon reversing the bias voltage, leading to a π phase shift in the topography along the same line cut between opposite bias voltages. We attributed this behavior to spin-selective tunneling of the SmB 6 tip, while the underlying mechanism is not resolved yet [49]. By analogy, the 

π phase shift in QPIs between the + x and −y directions on KV 2Se 2O actually reveals spin splitting in momentum space, as the standing waves are induced by interference between electrons that connect by wavevector qx or qy (see the inset of Fig. 2C). Since spin-conserved scattering is dominant around impurities, the QPI signal forms cross-shape standing waves. Given the theoretically predicted d-wave type AM spin structure, the cross-shape LDOS 9modulations should have opposite spin polarization between x and y directions. Therefore, the π phase shift only revealed by SmB 6-tip via spin-selective tunneling strongly indicates that dxz and dyz bands in KV 2Se 2O are oppositely spin-polarized in momentum space. While Figure 3 already provides real-space indications of altermagnetism, these observa-tions are limited to a single impurity and a specific phase shift. To establish the generality of this phenomenon, a more systematic analysis is required—one that accounts for the im-purity locations and types, as well as potential tip anisotropies. If the QPI signals are indeed oppositely spin-polarized between qx and qy directions, we expect that both the am-plitude and phase of the oscillations should differ markedly between the x and y directions. This characteristic anisotropy should be readily accessible with the spin-polarized SmB 6-tip. Most importantly, the anisotropy between qx and qy should reverse upon reversing the bias voltage-a hallmark of spin-polarized tunneling. In contrast, measurements with a spin-degenerate W-tip provide a baseline for comparison. With the W-tip, scattering from different impurities is detected without spin selectivity, so the measured QPI intensities should be similar between ±qx and ±qy. Furthermore, even if the W-tip itself possesses an intrinsic anisotropy, such an anisotropy would not reverse with bias voltage, distinguishing it from the spin-driven signal we seek. To get statistical evidence for d-wave altermagnetism, we extended our QPI analysis to a larger field of view containing multiple impurities up to 35 nm 2. Figure 4 presents a comprehensive comparison between measurements acquired with W- and SmB 6-tips at both positive and negative bias voltages. To quantify the anisotropy between orthogonal directions, we construct a difference map D(q) = I(q)−R·I(q), where I(q) is the QPI pattern at a given bias voltage and R is a π/ 2 rotation operator (see Figs. 4A,B and E,F). All maps are normalized by the intensity of the Bragg peaks. The raw real-space dI/dV maps and their corresponding FFT results, including the basal data for the ±20 mV analysis as well as extended measurements at ±30 mV, are detailed in the SM, Fig. S12. For the W tip, the difference maps reveal no clear asymmetry between qx and qy, irrespective of the bias voltage polarity (see Figs. 4C and D). In stark contrast, measurements with the SmB 6-tip yield a clear, bias-dependent anisotropy: D(q) exhibits opposite signs for qx and qy, and these signs reverse upon switching the bias voltage from positive to negative (see Figs. 4G and H). The spin contrast between qx and qy QPI peaks is much clearer than previous results, which is likely due to the high spin sensitively of SmB 6-tip observed previously [48]. The altermagnet KV 2Se 2O provides an ideal material platform to explore the interplay be-tween competing magnetic orders and their signatures in electronic excitations. In the bulk, KV 2Se 2O was found to form a G-type antiferromagnetic order below TN , in which the N´ eel vector alternates between adjacent layers [39]. This magnetic structure has recently been identified as a hidden altermagnetism, characterized by an additional symmetry operation [Us|Mz ], where Mz is the out-of-plane mirror reflection. Hence, each individual layer pos-sesses an AM character, leading to an even–odd layer dependence in few-layer samples [42]. This behavior is distinct from the previously explored even–odd effect in topological antifer-romagnetic MnBi 2Te 4, where the difference between even-layer and odd-layer systems lies in the presence or absence of net magnetization [54–59]. In contrast, the even-odd layer-parity dependence in the KV 2Se 2O family originates from the net AM character, as both even-layer 11 and odd-layer systems maintain zero net magnetization. Nevertheless, interlayer hybridiza-tion along the c-axis is extremely weak, rendering the system quasi-two-dimensional. Since STM is a highly surface-sensitive probe, only the topmost layer of KV 2Se 2O contributes to our measurements, effectively realizing the monolayer AM behavior. On the other hand, our work provides a method for investigating AM in real space. The SmB 6-nanowire probe not only delivers high spin resolution but also enables visualization of surface properties at the sub-nanometer scale. This capability allows us to resolve the spin structure within a single magnetic domain—a critical advantage over optical-based techniques, which average over macroscopic areas. Therefore, our SmB 6-tip-based STM is applicable to detect the hidden AM even–odd effect through local spin-resolved spectroscopy. More broadly, this approach could be extended to probe magnetically compensated spin textures in a wide range of correlated and topological quantum materials. In summary, combining the normal metallic W-tip and topological SmB 6-nanowire tip, we provide unambiguous real-space and momentum space evidence for the AM spin splitting of KV 2Se 2O. Detailed information related to both the SDW gap and the d-wave type AM spin-splitting band structures are directly visualized. In addition, theoretical simulations based on a minimal d-wave AM model agree excellently with the QPI measurements. The identification of such an AM order in KV 2Se 2O opens new avenues to explore exotic physical phenomena arising from nonrelativistic lifted Kramers degeneracy, including the anomalous Hall and Nernst effects, nonrelativistic spin-polarized currents, the magneto-optical Kerr effect, and chiral magnon excitations. Looking forward, the unique properties of KV 2Se 2Omake it a promising candidate for spintronics applications, such as enabling efficient spin current generation without net magnetization, tunable magnetic tunnel junctions with even-odd layer-dependent responses, and ultra-low dissipation magnonic devices. Moreover, its van der Waals layered structure and robust AM order, combined with strong correlation physics, position KV 2Se 2O as a versatile building block for ultra-compact spintronic devices, magnetic tunnel junctions, and quantum sensing technologies.
