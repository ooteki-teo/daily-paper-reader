(Dated: April 1, 2026) The subject of the present paper is a detailed theoretical investigation of the energy spectrum and bandgaps, as well as collective properties and linear response, in d-wave altermagnets in the presence of an off-resonance optical dressing field. We consider the altermagnets with both dx2−y2

and dxy pairing symmetries and focus on anisotropic dressing fields applied to an anisotropic and non-linear electron Hamiltonian. We have uncovered several crucial properties of the resulting electron-dressed state; specifically, we found that a finite bandgap is opened by linearly polarized irradiation, a phenomenon not observed in Dirac materials. A number of crucial properties of the electron dressed states in the presence of the linearly polarized light can be uncovered only in the second-order perturbation expansion, which is often omitted. We demonstrate that introducing an anisotropic driving field leads to several subtle yet important changes in the Edelstein susceptibilities of alternagents, enabling the fine-tuning of their spin polarizations. All these results must be in high demand due to the rapidly developing fields of spintronics and device physics. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

Altermagnetism is a recently discovered magnetic phase (a distinct state of matter) which is neither ferromagnetic nor conventional antiferromagnetic, but combines most crucial features of both. 1–6 Specifically, its net magnetic moment is zero, yet the electronic band structure shows spin splitting – a property which is usually observed in ferromagnets. 4,7 Thus, altermagnetism introduces a revolutionary paradigm in magnetic materials by breaking the traditional divisions and merging characteristics traditionally attributed to all the known types of magnetic ordering. The magnetic moments of altermagnets on different sublattices are antiparallel, which leads to the cancellation of the net magnetization, but the crystal symmetry is such that the electronic states on the two sublattices are not equivalent, producing momentum-dependent spin polarization 8 (see also Fig. 1 ( a)). This leads to a large spin-splitting of the electron energy subbands without net magnetization and efficient spin-resolved transport which could result in potential applications in low-power spintronics, spin-orbit–free spin control, and high- temperature devices. The spin splitting of the band structure of the altermagnets is symmetry-protected, which demonstrates a direct relation to the topological phases of the electronic structure of the corresponding materials. The exist a noticeable list of experimentally confirmed altermagnets, 9–11 specifically a layered intercalated transition-metal dichalcogenide, CoNb 4Se8, KV 2Se 2O, RbV 2Te 2O 12–14 and thin films of manganese telluride, MnTe, 2,15,16 

which have clearly exhibited altermagnetic band-structure properties. Researchers have also employed first-principles calculations, such as density functional theory, to propose a wide variety of materials that, in principle, should exhibit alternamagnetism, including FeS-based semiconductors 4. Importantly, several ordinary materials (oxides, pnictides, etc.) actually demonstrate altermagnetic symmetry, but we only recently realized it. This opens a large library of materials for new technological applications, and gaining new knowledge about their electronic and magnetic structure is crucial. Also, CrSb has been predicted and increasingly studied as an altermagnetic semiconductor. 17,18 

Spin–orbit coupling (SOC) 19–22 plays an important role in altermagnets since the spin splitting arises from crystal and magnetic symmetries. Often induced by external electrostatic voltage gate, it could modify and hybridize spin texture, as well as enable anomalous Hall responses, spin–orbital textures and related phenomena. 23–26 Also, there are topological spin persistance currents, 27–29 orbital magnetization 30 related to spin-orbit coupling.

Altermagnetic order may enable directional singlet pairing, generating spin mixtures, which allow topological su-perconducting phases due to the coupling between symmetry-protected spin structures. In altermagnetic systems superconductivity coexists with other types of pairing states. This superconductivity could be very stable since altermagnets do not exhibit a net macroscopic magnetization. 35–37 

Floquet engineering is a dynamical, tunable, and controllable modification of the key electronic properties – mostly band structure and the corresponding band gaps – in various two-dimensional materials and electronic surface states. 38–41 . It is achieved by applying a high-frequency, periodic optical driving field. 42 In contrast to strong, ionizing irradiation, we consider high-frequency dressing with energies (frequencies) well above any characteristic energy of the system, such as the Fermi energy. Theoretically, these new bound electron-photon states, or quasiparticles, are considered a very new kind of field-matter structures and are modeled by time-periodic perturbations of the coherent dressing of matter. Once the dressing field is applied, the band structure of the considered materials is modified in a non-trivial way. 43 

In principle, the results of applying the driving field substantially depend on its polarization. The most well-known result is opening a sizable bandgap by circularly polarized light 44–49 A linearly polarized light leads to the creation or modification of existing anisotropy 50,51 and asymmetric Klein tunneling 52,53 in various materials with the Dirac cone. All essential transport properties, such as inverse realization time and components of the Fermi velocities of a material, are changed 54–57 Importantly, plasmons and other collective effects 58–60 could be also substantially modified by the optical driving field 61 Applying external dressing filled with different polarizations could also substantially modify the RKKY interaction. 62 in altermagnets. 63 

Most of the crucial aspects of Floquet theory and Floquet engineering for graphene and other direct materials have been successfully experimentally realized, including both the induction of nonequilibrium band structures and the emergence of topological properties. The most crucial and commonly used methods for the experimental investiga-tion of Floquet states have been pump–probe and time- and angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy (tr-ARPES) measurements. This experiments demonstrated most crucial results in the theory of electron dressed states, including demonstrated light-induced band renormalization and the opening of dynamical gaps at the Dirac points 64,65 , Floquet band crossings, accompanied by coherent Floquet sidebands 66,67 A detailed comparison between the theoretical and experimental results are provided in Ref. [68]. Recently, ultrafast optical driving was applied to engineer transient Berry curvature and anomalous Hall responses. 69,70 

Apart from the changes to the energy spectrum and band gaps, optical driving fields have become powerful and widely used tools to induce and control topological phases in graphene and related materials. Opening a gap re-sults in breaking the time-reversal symmetry and creating a Haldane-like Chern insulator with light-tunable band topology 71,72 , higher-order topology, and dynamical band inversion in transition metal dichalcogenides 73,74 . Periodic driving provides a versatile nonequilibrium tool to design topological band structures 75 and many-body phase tran-sitions in low-dimensional materials exposed to such a dressing field. 76–80 . It also modifies the topological properties of its Fermi surface, known as the Lifshitz phase transition. 81,82 

Finally, optical dressing field could be applied to generate desirable structures of spin polarization, spin texture, topology and spin-orbit coupling 83–86 , RKKY interaction 63,87,88 in various low-dimensional lattices, including alter-mangets. The remaining part of the present paper is organized as follows: we begin with presenting a complete formalism for the low-energy Hamiltonian, electronic states and the corresponding energy spectrum of altermagnets with dx2−y2

and dxy pairing symmetries in Section II. We also discuss the proposed experimental setup for the considered two-dimensional altermagnetic material with gate voltage induced spin-orbit coupling and external off-resonance high-frequency dressing field. Section III is concerned with presenting our results for the electron dressed states for altermagnet exposed to various types of an isotropic dressing field with β̸ = 1. We discuss in detail all the novel properties in contrast to the previously considered circularly polarized light. The detailed derivations of all these electronic states, energy spectrum, and bandgaps is presented in Appendix B. Next, we calculate and present the detailed numerical results for different types of energy dispersions, as well as their constant-energy cuts (showing the angular dependence of the obtained spectrum) in Section IV. Finally, in section V, we provide the final concluding remarks, emphasizing the significance of our findings. The research outlook and potential future directions are discussed , highlighting the importance of the original results for the field and inspiring further investigation. Acomprehensive, unified expression for calculating energy dispersions for all types of spin-1/2 Hamiltonian, which is also applicable to qubit states, is presented and explained in Appendix A. 3

We consider anisotropic types of the light polarization, specifically, elliptical ( β < 1) and linear ( β = 0). The results for the energy spectrum for an irradiated altermagent with finite and zero spin-orbit coupling are presented in Figures 4 and 5, correspondingly. Most importantly, a finite bandgap between the two bands is generated in both cases. The angular dependence of these subbands are generally preserved in the presence of circularly polarized driving field. However, now we observe some very unusual types of dispersions with changing its concave-convex form with an increasing wave vector k. These dispersions are definitely a result of highly unusual interplay between the driving field and spin-orbit coupling. It is also interesting to notice that the distinction between the directions of out-of-plain polarization is no longer at ±π/ 4and ±3π/ 4 directions. These spin textures also becomes very different from separation of the two energy subbands corresponding to s = ±1. As an alternative, we also apply the elliptically polarized optical filled ways β = 0 .2 which is a lot closer to the linear polarization compared to the previously considered cases. We observe that a substantial band gap is still opened, which is in stark contrast with all the known Dirac cone materials, including graphene and the α − T 3 model. 91–93 

The angular dependence (anisotropy) of the energy spectrum in this case is also strongly affected, however, this effect is gap-related: the upper bands are much more elevated in the x−direction. We need to keep in mind that these calculations are performed within the first order of perturbation expansion which are in principle not applicable to the linearly polarized irradiation, corresponding to β = 0. This gives us an opportunity to compare what types of the additional Hamiltonian terms result in each separate effect (bandgap and anisotropy). Finally, we want to take a quick look at an altermagnet with a dxy symmetry. For β = 0 .9, we observe a noticeable bandgap and monotonic energy dispersions. However, its angular dependence is rather unusual with the subbands most extended along ±π/ 4 and ±3π/ 2 directions due to a kxky -term which present both in the initial Hamiltonian and in the radiation-induced additions. The separation between the positive and negative directions of out-of-plane spin polarizations correspond to 0 and π/ 2 angles, which has not been observed for any previous situations in dx2−y2

Therefore, the dependence on the anisotropy of the dressing field is not monotonic and limiting cases of circularly and linearly polarized light both show a substantial bandgap. The bandgap is also a monotonically increasing function of spin-orbit coupling ρ. However, this dependence becomes substantial only for β̸ = 0, i,e., next to the limit of circularly polarized irradiation of the dressing field. Finally, we investigate the energy dispersion and they are angular dependent for the case of literally polarized light. As we are now aware, we cannot limit our consideration to the first order of the perturbation expansion, and the second-order corrections are needed. It is important to mention that the allowable intensity of light in the off-resonance regime could create electron light coupling parameter Kω ∽ 5.0 k(0) . V. SUMMARY AND CONCLUDING REMARKS 

In this paper, we investigated the electron energy spectrum and bandgaps for d-wave altermagnets in the presence of an off-resonant optical dressing field and gate-induced spin-orbit coupling. The materials with both dx2−y2 and 

dxy symmetries have been investigated. The optical driving field leads to noticeable modifications of the electron properties and energy dispersions, resulting in substantially different electronic and collective behavior of the consid-ered materials. Specifically, a modification of the Fermi velocity components leads to a completely different situation in quantum transport, specifically, the Boltzmann conductivity. The results of applying external irradiation depend substantially on its polarization: circularly polarized light opens a bandgap, significantly modifying the material’s topological properties, and linearly polarized light creates or modifies the material’s energy spectrum. We have uncovered highly unusual and physically rich picture of the electron dressed states in both dx2−y2 and dxy 

altermagnets which is completely different from that in the previously investigated Dirac cone materials. We have found that a finite bandgap between the two bands is generated by elliptically polarized light, disregarding the spin-orbit coupling parameter. We observe both direct and indirect gaps along the x-direction, whereas the indirect gap along the y-axis is missing. It is also interesting to notice that the distinction between the directions of out-of-plain polarization is no longer at ±π/ 4 and ±3π/ 4 directions. When we apply a strongly anisotropic elliptically polarized optical field with β = 0 .2, we observe that a substantial band gap is still opened, which is in stark contrast with all the known Dirac cone materials. We also look at an altermagnet with a dxy symmetry: its separation between the positive and negative directions of out-of-plane spin polarizations corresponds to 0 and π/ 2 angles; such a behavior has not been found in dx2−y2 altermagnet. We observe that a linearly polarized dressing field yields a substantial energy bandgap in contrast to all known Dirac materials. Also, the angular dependence of the energy spectrum is 17 modified only in one direction (perpendicular to the light polarization direction θ0 = 0 .0). We also investigate the generation of in-plane spin polarization due to external magnetic fields in various types of altermagnets, known as Edelstein susceptibilities, or the Edelstein effect, in the presence of an off-resonance dressing field. We emphasize anisotropic polarization of the external irradiation to explore this effect in the case of anisotropic radiation interacting with electrons, which also exhibit anisotropic, non-linear energy dispersion. We have investigated how susceptibilities depend on two crucial parameters – the electron-light interaction and the electron spin-orbit coupling strength– in the presence of a driving field with β < 1. We have found out that even though the general trends and dependences remain unaltered, introducing an anisotropic driving field leads to several subtle but important changes of χxy , which allows for the fine-tuning of spin polarizations in a d-wave altermagents. This situation is very different for the altramagnets with dxy - type symmetry. Specifically, χxy is always zero in the absence of irradiation for any spin-orbit coupling ρ. This result stands in stark contrast to previously considered dx2−y2 alternates, in which the χxy tensor components reach their largest values in the absence of the dressing field. Altermagnets are considered most promising candidates for spintronics 94,95 because they merge the most prominent characteristics of ferromagnets and antiferromagnets, allowing efficient spin current control without stray fields that hinder device scaling. 1,3,96–99 They also display strong spin-dependent transport effects, such as anisotropic spin Hall responses and magnetoresistance, enabling straightforward electrical detection. 100,101 We strongly believe that our findings on the unusual electronics states, energy spectrum, dispersions and bandgaps for irradiated altermagnets which could be dynamically tuned, are very important for a number of practical applications, including spintronics and device physics.
