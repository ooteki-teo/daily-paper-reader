Altermagnets provide a promising platform for unconventional transport and optical responses beyond conventional ferromagnets and antiferromagnets. In this work, we develop a medium-throughput first-principles workflow to evaluate transport and optical properties in approximately 150 known altermagnetic compounds collected from the MAGNDATA database. By combining den-sity functional theory, Wannier interpolation, and symmetry analysis, we investigate representative linear and nonlinear responses, including the anomalous Hall effect, magneto-optical Kerr effect, and bulk photovoltaic effect. We find that these responses are strongly constrained by magnetic symme-try and further shaped by spin–orbit coupling, band structure, and inversion symmetry breaking. Representative examples include a finite anomalous Hall response in metallic VNb 3S6, giant Kerr rotation in insulating CaIrO 3, and large shift current in non-centrosymmetric CuFeS 2. These re-sults establish a symmetry-guided route for identifying experimentally accessible fingerprints and functional transport properties in altermagnetic materials. 

INTRODUCTION 

Altermagnetism has recently emerged as a new fron-tier in condensed matter physics, establishing a mag-netic paradigm beyond the conventional distinction be-tween ferromagnets and antiferromagnets. Although altermagnets exhibit vanishing net magnetization, as do conventional antiferromagnets, they host pronounced momentum-dependent spin splitting even in the absence of spin–orbit coupling (SOC), a property more commonly associated with ferromagnets.[1–3] This unusual coexis-tence of compensated magnetism and spin-polarized elec-tronic structure renders altermagnets attractive for spin-tronic and optoelectronic applications because of their negligible stray fields, robustness against magnetic cross-talk, and electronically active spin splitting. Since the defining electronic signatures of altermagnets are dic-tated by symmetry, their identification and classifica-tion require an appropriate symmetry framework. While magnetic space groups have traditionally been used to classify magnetic crystals, recent work has shown that spin space groups, in which spin and lattice degrees of freedom are decoupled, provide a more natural de-scription of altermagnetic spin splitting.[4] Motivated by these unique symmetry and electronic properties, high-throughput computational searches have rapidly ex-panded the family of candidate altermagnets. For ex-ample, advanced workflows combining density functional theory (DFT) and many-body methods have uncov-ered previously unknown metallic altermagnets,[5] while symmetry-based analyses rooted in spin space group the-ory have identified numerous candidate systems host-ing unconventional spin-split excitations, with the spin splitting often maximized away from high-symmetry paths.[6–8] However, despite these significant advances in symmetry classification and electronic structure char-acterization, the associated topological transport phe-nomena, particularly in the linear and nonlinear response regimes, remain largely unexplored. Closing this gap requires a framework that treats sym-metry, electronic structure, and transport on equal foot-ing. In this regard, the unconventional symmetry and electronic structure of altermagnets make them a natu-ral platform for topological transport phenomena. More generally, many observable responses in solids can be traced to the quantum geometry of Bloch states, in-cluding the Berry curvature, Berry-curvature dipole, and quantum metric,[9–11] whose allowed forms are strictly constrained by crystal and magnetic symmetries. In altermagnets, this geometric perspective is particularly compelling: the absence or breaking of combined sym-metries such as PT , together with the transformation properties of time-reversal and spin-space operations, can strongly constrain the allowed Berry curvature, quantum metric, and their higher-order moments. Altermagnets therefore provide an ideal setting in which to explore how symmetry and magnetic order reshape the phase texture of Bloch wave functions, and how this reshap-ing is encoded in experimentally observable transport and optical responses. This connection is most directly reflected in the linear-response regime, where phenom-ena such as the anomalous Hall effect (AHE),[12, 13] anomalous Nernst effect (ANE),[14, 15] magneto-optical Kerr effect (MOKE),[16, 17] and x-ray magnetic circu-lar dichroism (XMCD)[18, 19] arise from Berry curva-ture and related geometric quantities, and therefore obey closely related symmetry constraints. Beyond linear re-sponse, altermagnets also host a rich variety of nonlinear transport effects, including the shift current,[20–22] injec-tion current,[23, 24] and nonlinear Hall effect,[25] which are linked to higher-order geometric quantities such as the Berry-curvature dipole and the quantum metric. In this sense, altermagnets are not merely a new magnetic phase but also a symmetry-engineered platform for re-alizing and controlling a broad spectrum of topological, optical, and spintronic functionalities.

In this work, we develop a medium-throughput first-principles framework for the unified exploration of topo-logical transport phenomena in altermagnets. Starting from a symmetry-curated set of known altermagnetic compounds, we combine density functional theory, auto-mated Wannier interpolation, and symmetry analysis to investigate representative linear and nonlinear intrinsic responses, including the AHE, ANE, MOKE, and bulk photovoltaic effect (BPVE). Beyond identifying candi-date materials with pronounced response signatures, our aim is to uncover how altermagnetic order, quantum ge-ometry, and symmetry constraints jointly generate ex-perimentally accessible fingerprints. By analyzing these responses from the complementary viewpoints of group theory, we show that transport and optical observables offer a practical route to identify and utilize altermag-netism in real materials. This framework further provides symmetry-guided design principles for the discovery of high-performance altermagnetic compounds. 

> RESULTS AND DISCUSSION Workflow

To systematically evaluate transport and optical re-sponses in altermagnetic materials, we constructed a screening workflow based on first-principles calcula-tions and automated Wannier interpolation, as illus-trated in Fig. 1. Our starting point is the MAGN-DATA database,[26] which provides experimentally re-ported crystal structures and magnetic configurations. From this database, we collected 203 altermagnetic com-pounds previously identified by DFT calculations and classified by spin space group.[7, 25] After excluding 50 non-stoichiometric entries, 153 stoichiometric com-pounds were retained for further study. All candi-date materials were first examined by DFT+ U calcu-lations including SOC. The treatment of correlated or-bitals was adapted to the magnetic constituents of each compound. For transition-metal-based compounds with-out rare-earth elements, Hubbard corrections were ap-plied directly to the localized d states. For rare-earth-containing systems in which magnetism is dominated by transition-metal atoms, such as REFeO 3, RENiO 3, and REMnO 3, the rare-earth 4 f electrons were treated using the 4 f -in-core approximation. By contrast, the 4 f states were explicitly included in the valence when rare-earth moments play a dominant role in the magnetic order or jointly contribute with transition-metal atoms. After convergence tests on total energies, atomic forces, and magnetic moments, 18 compounds were discarded, leav-ing 135 converged candidates. We then carried out au-tomated maximally localized Wannier function (MLWF) construction using an in-house wannierization workflow interfacing VASP and Wannier90.[27] Among the 135 converged systems, three were excluded at this stage because the memory requirements exceeded the avail-able computational resources, and 132 compounds even-tually yielded well-converged Wannier representations. These Wannier Hamiltonians provide the common ba-sis for evaluating the response functions discussed be-low. Metallic systems were analyzed in terms of Berry-curvature-driven linear responses, including the AHE and ANE. Insulating candidates were further examined for the MOKE, while non-centrosymmetric compounds were selected for second-order shift-current calculations. In this way, the workflow links the symmetry classification of altermagnets to the corresponding linear and nonlin-ear transport observables and provides the basis for the material-specific analyses presented in the following sec-tions.

Most regions of the two-dimensional Brillouin zone contribute positively, consistent with the sign of the integrated AHC, while the coexistence of pos-itive and negative regions reflects the symmetry-imposed Berry-curvature texture of the altermagnetic state. Since the pure charge AHC is forbidden in the SOC-free spin-space-group limit, the finite σxy obtained here directly demonstrates that SOC converts the underlying alter-magnetic band splitting into an observable Hall signal by generating a nonvanishing Berry-curvature distribu-tion. These results establish VNb 3S6 as a representative metallic altermagnet with pronounced transverse trans-port responses and illustrate how SOC and symmetry to-gether govern the emergence of Hall and thermoelectric effects in metallic altermagnets. 

> Insulating altermagnets

In contrast to the metallic systems discussed above, where the AHC can be directly evaluated at the Fermi level, insulating compounds require a different approach to probe transverse responses. The MOKE response is governed not only by magnetic symmetry but also by the SOC-induced reconstruction of the band structure and the resulting redistribution of interband optical transi-tion strength. It therefore provides a powerful route for accessing transverse responses in insulating systems. The symmetry-allowed Kerr tensor components are fixed by the magnetic space group, while the magnitude of the Kerr rotation shows a strong material dependence. Among all insulating candidates, CaIrO 3 stands out as an exceptional case, exhibiting the largest Kerr rota-tion in our dataset, with a symmetry-allowed θzx compo-nent reaching a maximum of 3 .5◦. This unusually large response is closely related to the spin–orbit-entangled 

jeff = 1 /2 physics often realized in 5 d transition-metal 5oxides.[34–36] In such systems, the low-energy electronic structure emerges from the cooperative action of SOC, crystal-field splitting, and electron correlation. SOC en-tangles the t2g manifold into effective jeff = 1 /2 and 3 /2states, while the Hubbard interaction further opens a gap in the half-filled jeff = 1 /2 sector, leading to a spin–orbit-assisted Mott-insulating state. Optical transitions from valence states with mixed jeff = 3 /2 and 1 /2 charac-ter to unoccupied states dominated by jeff = 1 /2 can then generate pronounced Kerr rotation signals in the visible range. In realistic compounds, octahedral tilt-ing and rotation distort the ideal cubic crystal field, mix the t2g states, and drive the system away from the pure 

jeff = 1 /2 limit. In altermagnetic insulators, these dis-tortions play an additional role by breaking the combined 

PT symmetry and enabling momentum-dependent spin splitting. CaIrO 3 crystallizes in a post-perovskite structure com-posed of corner-sharing IrO 6 octahedra, as shown in Fig. 4(a). The octahedra exhibit alternating tilting of approximately ±23 ◦ along the z axis, forming zigzag Ir–O–Ir chains. This structural distortion not only mod-ifies the crystal-field environment but also breaks the combined PT symmetry, thereby enabling altermagnetic spin splitting along specific momentum directions. First-principles calculations show that SOC plays a decisive role in CaIrO 3. While the non-relativistic band struc-ture already exhibits momentum-dependent spin split-ting along the X1 → A1 path shown in Fig. 4(b), consis-tent with spin-space-group analysis predicting altermag-netic splitting along k = ( −0.27 , 0.72 , k z ), the inclusion of SOC [Fig. 4(d)] opens a band gap of about 0.5 eV and lifts the remaining degeneracies. Notably, the alter-magnetic spin splitting develops predominantly along the 

kz direction, whereas the SOC-induced spin polarization mainly lies in the kx–ky plane, highlighting a separation between altermagnetic and SOC-induced spin textures in both momentum and spin space. Although the octahedral distortions drive CaIrO 3 away from the ideal jeff = 1/2 limit, its low-energy elec-tronic structure still retains the essential features of a spin–orbit-assisted Mott insulator. This is reflected in the mixed orbital character near the Fermi level and the sizable orbital polarization shown in Fig. 4(e). The re-sulting Kerr spectrum in Fig. 4(c) exhibits a giant θzx 

response, reaching 3 .5◦ around 1.23 eV and 1 .26 ◦ around 1.5 eV. These peaks originate from interband transitions between valence states with partial jeff = 3 /2 character and conduction states dominated by jeff = 1 /2, which generate strong circular dichroism, as shown in Fig. 4(f). The MOKE response in such materials should therefore be understood as the result of several intertwined ingredi-ents: SOC, Hubbard correlation, crystal-field distortion, and altermagnetic spin splitting.

> Inversion-asymmetric altermagnets

Beyond the Berry-curvature-driven linear responses discussed above, inversion-asymmetric altermagnets pro-vide a natural platform for exploring nonlinear effects as-sociated with quantum geometry. When spatial inversion symmetry is broken, the real-space polarization structure of the crystal can couple to the altermagnetic electronic structure and generate nonlinear photocurrents. A pro-totypical example is the BPVE, a second-order nonlinear optical phenomenon that converts light directly into a dc current in a homogeneous crystal without requiring a p–n junction or interface.[37, 38] One important mi-croscopic mechanism of the BPVE is the shift current, which originates from the real-space shift of the elec-tron charge center during inversion-asymmetric optical excitation, thereby generating a dc photocurrent.[39–41] Among the altermagnetic compounds derived from the MAGNDATA dataset, we identify 22 systems without in-version symmetry. CONCLUSION 

In summary, we have established a medium-throughput first-principles framework for systematically evaluating linear and nonlinear transport responses in altermagnetic materials. By combining density func-tional theory, automated Wannier interpolation, and symmetry analysis, we show that the observable trans-port and optical properties of altermagnets are gov-erned by the interplay among magnetic symmetry, spin–orbit coupling, quantum geometry, and material-specific electronic structure. For insulating altermagnets, we show that strong spin–orbit coupling and favorable in-terband transitions can produce pronounced magneto-optical Kerr responses, with CaIrO 3 providing a repre-sentative case of a giant symmetry-resolved MOKE sig-nal. For inversion-asymmetric altermagnets, we identify several compounds, including CuFeS 2, Fe 2Mo 3O8, and VNb 3S6, that exhibit large shift-current responses com-parable to or even exceeding those of benchmark bulk photovoltaic materials. More broadly, our work shows that altermagnetic transport is not defined by a single re-sponse channel, but by a broader landscape of symmetry-selective observables that can be activated, enhanced, and tuned through magnetic configuration, spin–orbit coupling, inversion breaking, and lattice control. This provides both a practical strategy for identifying experi-mentally accessible fingerprints of altermagnetism and a design route toward multifunctional quantum materials for spintronic, magneto-optical, and photovoltaic appli-cations.

All first-principles calculations were performed within DFT using the projector augmented-wave (PAW) method[49] as implemented in VASP.[50] The exchange– correlation functional was treated within the generalized gradient approximation (GGA) in the Perdew–Burke– Ernzerhof (PBE) form.[51] SOC was included in all cal-culations. A Γ-centered k mesh with a reciprocal-space resolution of 0 .02 ˚ A−1 was used for Brillouin-zone sam-pling, and the plane-wave cutoff energy was set to 550 eV. Correlated states were treated within the DFT+ U frame-work.
