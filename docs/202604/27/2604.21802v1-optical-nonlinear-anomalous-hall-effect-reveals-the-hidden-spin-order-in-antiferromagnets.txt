Reading antiferromagnetic order remains a central obstacle for antiferromagnetic 

memory and logic because zero net magnetisation precludes conventional magnetic 

readout. Domain imaging typically relies on x -ray magnetic linear dichroism (XMLD) 

microscopy a t synchrotron sources, but XMLD is even under time reversal and cannot 

distinguish 180° -reversed magnetic states. Here we report the first experimental 

observation of the optical nonlinear anomalous Hall effect, predicted for antiferromagnets 

with combined parity – time -reversal (𝓟𝓣 ) symmetry. The effect stems from light -induced 

interband electric -dipole transitions, where spin -orbit coupling induces an asymmetry 

between  ±𝐤  states and generates a time -reversal -odd photocurrent whose sign flips upon 

180° reversal of the Néel vector . In  𝓟𝓣 -symmetric CuMnAs, we use near -field excitation 

to map this photocurrent with sub -100 -nm spatial resolution after current -induced spin -

orbit -torque switching. The signal polarity follows local Néel vector reversal, enabling 

nanoscale imaging of antiferromagnetic texture and direct readout of 180° -reversed 

antiferromagnetic states that remain indistinguishable in XMLD and other time -reversal -

even linear -dichroic probes. The optical n on linear anomalous Hall effect thus reveals a 

new light -spin interaction and provides a scalable route to nanoscale readout of hidden 

spin order, with potential for ultrafast all -electrical and all -optical antiferromagnetic 

spintronic technologies. Introduction 

Antiferromagnets are increasingly recognised as promising platforms for next -generation 

spintronic devices because they combine ultrafast dynamics, robustness against external 

perturbations and compatibility with dense integration. Their zero net magnetisation suppresses 

stray dipolar fields and enables compact device architectures, while strong exchange 

interactions support dynamics in the terahertz range, orders of magnitude faster than in 

ferromagnets. Together, these attributes make antiferromagnets att ractive building blocks for 

non -volatile, energy -efficient memory technologies 1-11 .

A central challenge for antiferromagnetic memory is the reliable readout of  180° -reversed order 

in nanoscale domains, in which information is encoded in oppositely oriented Néel vectors, 

+𝐍  and  −𝐍 . This binary encoding is especially attractive because the two states are related by 

time reversal and are energetically degenerate. In a uniaxial antiferromagnet, switching between 

+𝐍  and  −𝐍  cannot occur continuously without passing through a hard -axis configuration, so 

reversal is protected by a large anisotropy barrier. Destabilising the easy -axis state typically 

requires fields on the order of the exchange -enhanced spin -flop threshold, often several tesla 9-

> 13

. This intrinsic stability makes  ±𝐍  domains a natural basis for non -volatile information 

storage. 

The readout problem, however, is fundamentally different from that in ferromagnetic 

spintronics. In ferromagnets,  180°  reversal is readily detected by linear magnetotransport, 

magneto -optical, and thermomagnetic probes such as the anomalous Hall effect, the Kerr and 

Faraday effects, magnetic circular dichroism, and the anomalous Nernst effect. In conventional 

collinear antiferromagnets, by contrast, these familiar linear -response signatures are 

suppressed, making the direct detection of reversed domains intrinsically difficult. 

Experimentally, antiferromagnetic domains are therefore most commonly imaged by x -ray 

magnetic linear dichroism (XMLD) microscopy at synchrotron sources. Although powerful, 

XMLD is infrastructure -intensive and, because it is even under time reversal, yields identical 

contrast for  +𝐍  and  −𝐍  . More accessible probes, such as anisotropic magnetoresistance 

measurements and magneto -Seebeck microscopy, can also image Néel vector reorientation in 

collinear antiferromagnets 3, 14 -16, but these linear -response signals are likewise even in the order 

parameter and therefore insensitive to 180° Néel reversal. 

This invisibility, however, is not universal across compensated spin systems; rather, it is 

dictated by symmetry. Recent work on altermagnets has shown that symmetry -constrained 

exchange splitting can generate a momentum -dependent spin polarization that alternates 

between symmetry -related crystal directions while the net magnetisation remains zero, and 

moreover, can exhibit time -reversal -odd linear responses in appropriate crystal classes. 

Experimentally, spontaneous anomalous Hall and anomalous Nernst resp onses have already 

been observed in altermagnetic Mn 5Si 3, demonstrating that compensated collinear order need 

not be invisible in linear response 17-22 .

By contrast, the antiferromagnets relevant here belong to a different symmetry class:  𝒫𝒯  -

symmetric antiferromagnets. Combined parity – time -reversal symmetry enforces Kramers -like band degeneracy and eliminates Berry curvature at each  𝐤 -point, thereby suppressing Berry -

curvature -driven linear Hall signals and related time -reversal -odd responses. 

A route around this limitation is provided by second -order nonlinear Hall effects, which can 

arise without an external magnetic field in non -centrosymmetric crystals. In nonmagnetic WTe 2

and MoTe 2 the effect is governed by a Berry -curvature dipole 23 -25. In  𝒫𝒯  -symmetric 

antiferromagnets, however, the Berry curvature vanishes identically, so the Berry -curvature -

dipole mechanism is forbidden. Instead, nonlinear Hall -like signals can emerge from higher -

order geometric quantities governed by the quantum metric tensor, often discussed in terms of 

the Berry -connection polarizability, which remain finite and couple directly to the Néel vector 

orientation 24, 2 6-29 .

This second -order effect was first observed experimentally in our earlier work on CuMnAs ,

where the electrical nonlinear anomalous Hall response provided a transport signal that is odd 

in the Néel -order 30 . Subsequent experiments and theory established quantum -metric nonlinear 

Hall responses in additional  𝒫𝒯 -symmetric antiferromagnetic systems and placed these effects 

within a broader geometric framework for compensated antiferromagnets 31,3 2. More generally, 

magnetic point -group analysis shows that 48 of the 59 antiferromagnetic point groups that break 

time -reversal symmetry while forbidding a net moment also break inversion symmetry, making 

second -order, Néel -odd readout a broadly available symmetry route in antiferromagnets 30,3 3.

In this work, we extend this paradigm to the optical regime, which opens both a new window 

on light -spin interactions in antiferromagnets and a route to nanoscale -resolved readout 34-37. We 

show that linearly polarised infrared light incident on a  𝒫𝒯  -symmetric antiferromagnet 

generates a photocurrent whose sign is locked to the Néel vector orientation , a time -reversal 

odd phenomenon we term the optical nonlinear anomalous Hall effect (optical nl -AHE). Unlike 

its electrical analogue, this 2nd order response is driven by interband electric -dipole transitions, 

in which spin -orbit -coupling -induced band asymmetry produces a Néel -order -odd 

photocurrent. This distinguishes  𝒫𝒯  -symmetric collinear antiferromagnets from the  𝒞 𝑛 𝒯  -

symmetric altermagnetic class, for which the leading nonlinear transport response is of third 

order in electric field 23 and therefore cannot support Néel -order -dependent photocurrents. 

To resolve such photocurrents in  𝒫𝒯 -symmetric antiferromagnets on the scale of individual 

antiferromagnetic domains, we use a scattering -type scanning near -field optical microscop e (s -

SNOM), which concentrates the infrared field beneath a sharp metallic tip and locally excites 

the relevant interband transitions. The resulting photocurrent maps directly encode the local 

Néel vector orientation, enabling nanometre -resolved imaging of antiferromagnetic texture and 

direct discrimination between +  𝐍  and - 𝐍  domains, which remain indistinguishable in XMLD 

and other time -reversal -even linear -dichroic probes. The optical nl -AHE therefore provides a 

scalable optoelectronic route to non -invasive, ultrafast readout of hidden spin order in 

antiferromagnets. Results 

To demonstrate the optical nl‑AHE experimentally, we measure the near -field –induced optical 

nl -AHE voltage (oNA -voltage) in  ~20 𝑛𝑚  epitaxial films of the  𝒫𝒯  ‑symmetric 

antiferromagnet CuMnAs. The voltage measured at remote contacts reflects a photocurrent 

injected locally by the near -field excitation. Details about the device fabrication can be found 

in Supplementary Material 4. 

As shown in Fig. 1a, a metallic AFM tip illuminated with mid‑infrared light  (𝜆 = 10 μ 𝑚 )

concentrates the optical field into a nanoscale, strongly enhanced rotationally symmetric near 

field (Methods ). This near -field drives interband electric‑dipole transitions and generates a local 

photocurrent  𝒋 𝒐𝑵𝑨  via the optical nl‑AHE. For the high -symmetry in -plane states relevant here 

(N ∥ <110> (and likewise <100> ), the rotationally symmetric s -SNOM near -field yields a net 

photocurrent perpendicular to  𝑵  (ref. 34). Under continuous illumination, compensating return 

currents in the device redistribute charge until a steady‑state potential difference builds up 

between the external contacts, which we detect as the oNA‑voltage. 

Scanning the tip across a 1 μm‑wide CuMnAs bar yields a two‑dimensional oNA‑voltage map 

(Fig. 1b) that reveals the as‑grown antiferromagnetic domain pattern. As sketched in Fig. 1c, 

the signal polarity follows the Néel vector  𝑵  orientation through its net transverse component 

relative to the bar axis , leading to opposite voltage polarities across domain boundaries.

To verify the symmetry of the optical nl -AHE, we prepared well -defined magnetic states with 

controlled Néel vector orientations along  < 110 > . In narrow CuMnAs bars, the large 

as‑grown domains (Fig. 1b) are attributed to strain‑driven variations of the magnetic anisotropy 

arising from lattice‑mismatch relaxation, rather than dipolar interactions as in ferromagnets. 

Similar strain‑induced effects have been reported in patterned thin films with negligible dipolar 

coupling, including low‑moment diluted magnetic semiconductors 38 and antiferromagnets 39 .

The tendency towards large domains weakens with increasing device width. 

First, we use a  5 μ𝑚 ‑wide four‑terminal crossbar (Fig. 2a) that enables electrical writing and 

near‑field readout. Optical nl‑AHE detection is performed in an open‑circuit configuration, 

where the locally injected photocurrent is read out as an oNA‑voltage between remote conta cts 

(see Methods and Supplementary Material 1). We define  Δ𝑉 = (𝑉 𝐴 + − 𝑉 𝐴 − ) − (𝑉 𝐵 + − 𝑉 𝐵 − ) as 

the difference of the simultaneously recorded potential drops along the A and B axes, which 

corresponds to the net potential drop along the upper‑right to lower‑left diagonal (Fig. 2a). Th e

voltage values were extracted from the AC voltage signal s produced by the height modulation 

of the s‑SNOM tip, which isolates the near‑field contribution; the extraction procedure is 

detailed in Methods. 

In the relaxed ground state (Fig. 2b), the film forms nanoscale domains with Néel vectors 

aligned along different crystallographic axes 40 ,41 . Because the domain size is comparable to the 

near‑field excitation area  (~20 𝑛𝑚 ), the associated local photocurrents are expected to average 

out, yielding no resolvable domain contrast. Consistent with this, the as‑grown oNA‑voltage 

map (Fig. 2c) shows only a weak residual background, indicating that the nanoscale domains 

share a slig ht common component perpendicular to the upper -right to lower -left diagonal. 

To overwrite the nanoscale texture and create macroscopic areas with a defined Néel vector, 

we apply  ~10 𝑚𝑠  long current pulses of approximately  40 𝑚𝐴  along the upper‑right to 

lower‑left diagonal by shorting the contact pairs  (𝑉 𝐴 + , 𝑉 𝐵 − ) and  (𝑉 𝐴 − , 𝑉 𝐵 + ) . Simulations 

(Fig. 2d, g) show that the current density and correspondingly the staggered spin –orbit field 

peaks at the two inner corners transverse to the pulse direction (Supplementary Material 1).

Where the local current density exceeds the spin –orbit‑torque switching threshold, the Néel 

vector reorients perpendicular to the current 3, 10 ,30 , producing two opposite corners with parallel 

average Néel vector orientations (Fig. 2e). Reversing the pulse polarity reverses the Néel vector 

in the same regions, yielding a 180°‑switched configuration (Fig. 2h). 

After each pulse, we scan the illuminated s‑SNOM tip across the device and record the 

steady‑state oNA‑voltage using the same contact configuration. The resulting map (Fig. 2f) 

shows strong, corner‑localised signals at the current pulse‑affected regions, demonstrating that 

in the pulse -written regions the optical nl -AHE current follows the writing -current direction. 

Because spin –orbit -torque switching aligns the Néel vector perpendicular to the pulse, this is 

consistent with the Hall -like response expected for the high -symmetry <110> states in our 

rotationally symmetric near -field geometry. When the pulse polarity is reversed, the 

oNA‑voltage at both corners reverses sign (Fig. 2i), evidencing that the photocurrent is locked

We further record voltages on the orthogonal contact pairs, which detect photocurrents 

longitudinal with respect to the pulse‑aligned Néel vector (Supplementary Material 2,

Fig s. S2.1, S 2.2 ). Within our sensitivity, no longitudinal contribution originating from these 

current pulse affected corner areas is resolved . Together with the polarity reversal under Néel 

vector inversion, this establishes a Hall -like photocurrent for the pulse -written <110> states that 

is odd under time reversal 34 . The response is analogous to the anomalous Hall effect in 

ferromagnets, where the transverse Hall signal reverses under magnetisation reversal, and it demonstrates the optical nl‑AHE as a local, symmetry‑governed probe of Néel vector 

orientation. 

Finally, the corner signals disappear when the device is rescanned after ~17 hours 

(Supplementary Material 2, compare Fig s. S2.1c, d ). We attribute this to relaxation back 

towards the initial magnetic state composed of sub‑resolution nanoscale domains 41 .

metallic tip generates a strongly enhanced local field whose dominant component is oriented

Equation (1) describes the generation of photoexcited electron –hole pairs whose group velocit y

contributions do not cancel when the electronic structure lacks the symmetries that enforce 

𝐤 ⟷ −𝐤  compensation . In  𝒫𝒯 -symmetric antiferromagnets, inversion and time reversal are 

individually broken while their product is preserved , enabling a finite time‑reversal‑odd second‑order response tied to the Néel order (the corresponding photocurrent vanishes in the 

paramagnetic phase but can remain finite once antiferromagnetic order is established). 

In the above calculations, we neglect disorder -related scattering contributions such as skew 

scattering and side -jump processes, which can also contribute to nonlinear Hall responses. 

Disorder effects in  𝒫𝒯  -symmetric antiferromagnets, including skew -scattering -related 

nonlinear Hall and chiral photocurrent mechanisms, have recently been analysed theoretically 42 -

> 44

, but their quantitative weight under mid -infrared interband excitation in our near -field 

geometry remains to be established. We therefore focus on the clean -limit injection current of 

Eq. (1), whose magnitude scales linearly with the optically excited car rier lifetime  𝜏 .

To connect this microscopic picture with the experiment, we performed first -principles density 

functional theory calculations 45, focusing in particular on configurations with the Néel vector 

aligned along  [110 ], as in the switching experiments shown in Fig 2 . For  𝒌 ∥ 𝑵 , the calculated 

dispersion remains symmetric under  𝒌 → −𝒌 , whereas for  𝒌 ⟂ 𝑵  it exhibits a pronounced 

+𝒌  versus  −𝒌  asymmetry (Fig. 3a). This asymmetry yields a net injected photocurrent whose 

time -reversal -odd part is dominated by the component transverse to N. Consistently, the 

computed injection -current conductivity tensor (Fig. 3b) shows finite time -reversal -odd 

components only for current directions perpendicular to N, while the corresponding 

components parallel to N vanish within numerical accuracy. In combination with the 

rotationally symmetric near -field geometry discussed above, this explains the experimentally 

observed transverse net response without implying that the same relation must hold for an 

arbit rary in -plane Néel vector direction.

antiferromagnetic texture is strongly governed by microtwin/defect -line pinning 40,

(Supplementary Material 3). In such films, structural defect lines running along the in -plane 

⟨110 ⟩ directions correlate with, and constrain, the AF domain pattern reported by 

XMLD -PEEM. Our optical nl -AHE maps reproduce the characteristic stripe -like, 

defect -correlated domain morphology of these thick films, consistent with prior XMLD -PEEM 

observations 40, while additionally providing a time -reversal -odd contrast that discriminates 

180° -reversed  +𝐍 /−𝐍  domains that remain indistinguishable in time -reversal -even 

XMLD -PEEM. The benchmark data are shown in Supplementary Material 3, Fig. S 3.

Conclusion 

In summary, we report the experimental observation of a predicted light –spin interaction 

mechanism in the  𝒫𝒯  -symmetric antiferromagnet CuMnAs. Using mid -infrared near -field 

excitation, the optical nonlinear anomalous Hall effect generates a local transverse photocurrent 

that is odd under Néel reversal and therefore enables nanoscale imaging of the Néel -texture 

with direct sensitivity to 180° -reversed domains. Combining electrical Néel -spin –orbit -torque 

writing with first -principles calculations and device -level modelling, we show that for the 

symmetry -selected in -plane states probed here, the measured oNA -voltage is consistent with a 

Néel -order -odd photocurrent directed perpendicular to the local Néel vector. Optical nl -AHE thus offers laboratory -scale antiferromagnetic nanoscopy complementary to XMLD -based 

microscopy, while overcoming its fundamental insensitivity to  ±𝑵 . More generally, our results 

establish nonlinear Hall photocurrents as a route to read out hidden order in  𝒫𝒯 -symmetric 

antiferromagnets and motivate future ultrafast opto -electronic read/write concepts of 

antiferromagnetic order. References

Methods 

Microscopic theory of the optical nonlinear anomalous Hall effect . Density functional 

theory calculations were performed using the full -potential local -orbital (FPLO) code 45. The 

exchange -correlation potential was parameterized within the generalized gradient 

approximation (GGA) using the Perdew -Burke -Ernzerhof (PBE) functional 50. The calculations 

were carried out in a fully relativistic mode, solving the four -component Kohn -Sham -Dirac 

equation to account for spin -orbit coupling. The integration over the Br illouin zone was 

performed using the tetrahedron method with a 12×12×12 k -mesh grid. A spin -polarized setup 

was employed, initialized with magnetic moments on the Manganese (Mn) sites with 

orientation along  [110 ] direction. 

To calculate the magnetic injection current on a finer k -grid, we further employed Wannier 

functions to construct a tight -binding model. The projection window included the energy range 

from  −13 .45 𝑒𝑉  to  0 𝑒𝑉 . The basis set for the projection consisted of Cu 4s, 3d; Mn 4s, 3d; 

and As 4s, 4p orbitals. Finally, the magnetic injection current was calculated using the HopTB 

package 29 ,51. In these calculations, we adopted a dense k -grid of 200×200×200 and a Gaussian 

broadening of  0.01 𝑒𝑉 .

Experimental Setup . All measurements were performed with commercially available s -

SNOM setups (neasnom, attocube systems GmbH), depicted schematically in Extended Data 

Fig. 1. Mid -infrared laser light of  ~10 μ𝑚  wavelength from quantum cascade lasers (MIRcat, 

DRS Daylight Solutions) is focused on PtIr -coated atomic force microscopy tips (25PtIr200B -

H, Rocky Mountain Nanotechnology LLC), where near -fields are excited at the tip apex, which 

excite local photocurren ts as described in the main text. The resulting voltage is simultaneously 

measured at both pairs of remote contacts along the sample cross’ axes and amplified by SR560 

low noise amplifiers (Stanford Research Systems) at a factor of  5 × 10 4, along with the sample 

topography. Due to the small extent of the near -fields compared to the large focal spot size, s -

SNOM is plagued by significant background contributions. To suppress this background, the 

atomic force microscope is operated in tapping mode, i.e., the tip is near -resonantly oscillated 

above the sample at the scale of the near -field decay 52 . The photocurrent signal is then analyzed 

at higher harmonics of the tapping frequency, to which the spatially highly non -linear near -

fields contribute significantly more than the spatially, at the scale of the oscillation amplitude, 

essentially linear fa r-field background. In the mid -infrared, the background is generally 

suppressed sufficiently at the second harmonic, which we also confirmed in our ph otocurrent 

simulations. Extended Data Figure 1 | The used photocurrent nanoscopy setup is sketched. A mid -infrared

4. Device fabrication 

The CuMnAs thin film was grown on  (001 ) GaP substrate by molecular beam epitaxy. First, 

the native oxide was desorbed at  640° 𝐶  (measured optically by absorption edge) under P2 

overpressure. A  50 𝑛𝑚  thick GaP buffer was grown at  550° 𝐶  at growth rate of  1.13 𝑚𝑚 /𝑚𝑖𝑛 .

Subsequently, the sample was cooled down to  220° 𝐶  and a  20 𝑛𝑚  thick CuMnAs layer was 

grown (0.35 𝑛𝑚 /𝑚𝑖𝑛 ) with fluxes calibrated to form 1:1:1 stoichiometric films. We note that 

the As4 flux is fine -tuned to obtain the ideal stoichiometry, which then results to formation of 

films with conformal morphology and reduced defect density (in comparison to Ref. 12). After 

CuMnAs growth, the sample was cooled to around  −20° 𝐶  and a  3𝑛𝑚  thin film was deposited. 

The purpose is to prevent oxidation of the CuMnAs film at ambient conditions after natural 

formation of stable AlOx. 

The wafers were then patterned into Hall bar and cross devices by electron beam lithography 

with a positive A4 PMMA resist. The structures are defined by a wet chemical process, where 

the etchant is based on the diluted H 2PO 4 acid. The electrical contacts were fabricated via a lift -

off process using Cr/Au metallization with thicknesses of  4 𝑛𝑚  and  80 𝑛𝑚 , respectively. References
