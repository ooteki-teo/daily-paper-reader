# Novel Light-Induced States in Triangular Metallic Magnet 

Yao Wang 

School of Physcis and Optoelectronic Engineering, Shandong University of Technology, Shandong, 255000, China ∗

(Dated: April 8, 2026) Novel nonequilibrium states of magnet induced by light attract considerable attention both in nature of physics and apply. In this work, we systematically explore the electronic and magnetic states of a double-exchange model on a triangular lattice under the irradiation of circularly polarized continuous wave field, by means of molecular dynamics calculation. Several exotic nonequilibrium magnetic states are discovered, including a vortex state, long-range magnetic orders at the Γ and 

K/2, as well as quasi(dynamical)-long-range magnetic order at the K and M, respectively. Cor-respondingly, the evolution of electron bands and fillings are also uncovered. These results offer a promising candidate approach for the optical control of exotic magnetic and electronic states. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

Optical control of magnetism plays an increasingly im-portant role in both fundamental and applied physics owing to its high speed, ease of handling, tunability of strength and frequency, extra phase and the polariza-tion degree of freedom, and so on. Accessing all essen-tial degrees of freedom in magnets, e.g., charge, orbital, spin and lattice, the light has distinctive advantages to triggering magnetic phase transitions and even the emer-gence of hidden orders [1]. Besides, ultrafast optical con-trol can realize magnetic phase transitions on subpicosec-ond time scales, providing a key technical support for the development of high-speed spintronic devices and ultra-fast magnetic storage [2]. Pioneered by the ultrafast demagnetization of a Ni film [3], various photoinduced nonequilibrium phenom-ena have been proposed and demonstrated in the past three decades [4, 5], such as light-induced coherent mag-netization precession [6], magnetic phase transitions [7], and hidden orders [8]. In addition, light fields can also modulate other magnetism-related effects, including su-perconductivity [9], Floquet–Bloch states [10], the Kondo effect [11], heavy-fermion states [12], multiferroicity [13], and magnetoresistance [14]. Among the various physics, one of the most highly attractive and extensively studied families is double-exchange(DE) system, which is originally proposed by Zener, and later refined by Anderson and Hasegawa for ferromagnetic (FM) oxides [15, 16]. Especially in colos-sal magnetoresistance materials, lots of novel experimen-tal observations induced by light have been reported, in-cluding metal-insulator transition [17], time-scale separa-tion between spin and charge [18], charge and orbital or-der, lattice structural distortion [19] and orthorhombicity evolution [20], etc. Motivated by these findings, numer-ous physics unrealized in equilibrium have been proposed based on DE model and its extensions theoretically [21]. Both continuous wave(cw) and pulse light fields can in-

duce a FM to antiferromagnetic(AFM) phase transition on a square lattice, accompanied by narrowed electronic bands, a widened energy gap, and 1/4 filling electrons uniformly occupying the lower band[22, 23]. Cw light can also induce a 120 ◦ AFM phase transition on triangu-lar lattice [24]. Furthermore, nontrivial topological spin configurations have been proposed in various lattice ge-ometries. An AFM chain can be driven to generate spin chirality by a typical pulse [25]. Photoinduced topolog-ical spin texture with a finite Pontryagin number and vector chirality can also be found in a square metallic fer-romagnet [26]. S. Ghosh et al. excited the square antifer-romagnet using a circularly polarized pulsed light field, generating meron-antimeron pairs that persist for over 100 ps [27]. Counterintuitively, when longer-range elec-tronic hopping exists on a triangular lattice centrosym-metrically, chirality-nontrivial spin configurations could be induced by a linearly polarized field. However, most of these studies focus on a single pho-toinduced magnetic state. Thus, a question is raised: Can we modulate various electronic and magnetic states by varying the light field? Previously, attentions have been paid to the strong DE interaction. Intuitively, in this coupling regime it is more feasible for electrons to drive local magnetic moments, due to their negligible coupling with light. However, a strong DE interaction and a FM initial equilibrium state typically result in a wide electronic band gap in equilibrium, which hin-ders inter-band electron transitions when irradiated by light. Furthermore, in order to acquire multiple pho-toinduced phases, we opt for the triangular lattice due to its relatively complex equilibrium phase diagram in the space of the electron filling and the strength of DE interaction [28]. Considering these two factors, we in-vestigate the light-irradiated DE model in weak DE cou-pling regime on the triangular lattice. Only the impacts of amplitude, and frequency on the DE model are ex-amined, while several novel electron and magnetic states have been uncovered. The models and methods adopted are specifically presented in Section II. Our main results are shown in Section III. Section IV provides some dis-cussions and concludes this article, respectively.

> B. Ferromagnetic State

FM order appears across almost the entire A0 and ω

ranges considered in our calculations. Two FM cases where A0|a1| = 2 .5, ω/t = 3 , 12 are shown in Fig. 3. Both S(q, τ t = 2000) peaks at Γ, where S(Γ) > 0.9, as shown in Fig. 3(b). However, the electrons exhibit differ-ent behaviors in the two cases. Electrons are excited onto all energy bands almost evenly at weaker ω (Fig.3(a)), which resembles the system is heated up to a signifi-cant high temperature. As ω increases, electrons start to settle into the lower energy bands (Fig.3(c)). Irritated by higher-frequency light, the band broadens faster than it does for small ω, though the light hardly influences the electrons’ highest energy for both cases. Besides, Fig. 3(d) reveals S(Γ) grows more rapidly with increasing 

ω, even though the equilibrium ground state whose S(K)dominants in q space is suppressed as soon as the system is exposed to light for both cases. By carefully examin-ing the process of reaching stable FM order, we find that for small ω, the magnetic configuration goes through a swirling state (Fig. 4(a)), which is different from the case of large ω where FM interactions rapidly become domi-nant and directly align the local magnetic moments along the same direction. Comparing (a) with (b) of Fig. 3, we find that energy band broadening implies a FM interac-tion mediated by electrons between magnetic moments, which is evidenced not only in Subsec. III C and III E, but also in other light-irradiated systems [22, 24].

> E. Long-Range Order at K/2 and K ′/2

This long-range order is primarily characterized by maxima in S(q) at K/2 and K′/2 (Fig. 7(b)), corre-sponding to a 2 √3×2√3 magnetic unit cell containing 12 sublattices in real space (drawn in the inset of Fig. 7(c)). Upon photoexcitation, electrons rapidly populate higher bands, and the band width first increases sharply before narrowing back to a value close to the equilibrium one by 

τ t = 700 (Fig. 7(a)). The fillings of bands stabilize into four layers. Unlike earlier cases, all bands are shifted downward, especially for the highest level. Consistent with our previous observations, band broadening signals a FM tendency, which is corroborated by the behavior in Fig. 7(c) as well. As soon as the light arrives, S(Γ) is strongly enhanced while S(K) is immediately suppressed, peaks when S(K) vanishes, then decays rapidly. Concur-rently, S(K/2) grows slowly, eventually dominates, and establishes stable long-range order. The basis vectors of the magnetic unit cell are 2 a1 −a2 and 2 a2 −a1, as shown in Fig. 7(d). Within a unit cell, the moments display pronounced AFM correlations along 2 a1 − a2, whereas such correlations are negligible small along the other di-rection, in agreement with the reciprocal-space peaks in Fig. 7(b). This configuration induces a periodic modu-lation of χijk along a2 − a1, shown in Fig. 7(d), where 

χijk =⃗ Si · (⃗Sj ×⃗ Sk), calculated by magnetic moments on upright triangles with vertices i, j, k . Although no clear AFM order is observed along 2 a2 − a1, the chiralities 

χijk of the moments are opposite between two neighbor-ing triangles along both basis directions, as inferred from 

χijk and lattice geometry in Fig. 7(d). To the best of our knowledge, such a long-range order has not yet been reported on the triangular lattice.  

> F. Dynamical Long-Range Order at M

A long-range ordered state at M, occupying a very small area in the phase diagram, exhibits periodic oscil-lations. To assess its stability, we examine the state up to 

τ t = 4000. As shown in Fig. 8(a), two energy gaps open near ±2t after the system enters this state. The electron density is the highest in the lowest band, intermediate in the middle band, and the upper band is the narrowest with the lowest electron density. But such an electron dis-tributions across the three bands are relatively uniform comparing the ones of larger ω excitations in other cases. The band-edge dynamics further indicate an oscillation period much longer than the optical frequency. Fig. 8(b) displays a clear long-range order at the M and only very weak order at the Γ. Despite the oscillatory behavior of the electronic structure, S(M) does not exhibit peri-odic oscillations during illumination (Fig. 8 (d)), imply-ing that the spatial periodicity of the 4-sublattice state remains stable (shown in the inset of Fig. 8), which re-sembles the scalar chiral state reported in Ref. [30] and exhibits an anomalous Hall effect [31]. Thus dynami-cal interactions between moments mediated by oscillat-ing electrons modifies the magnetic configuration within a magnetic unit cell, manifesting as a weak oscillating FM order when the 4 moments approach and depart from the regular-tetrahedron limit, which is demonstrated by ¯χ and ¯θ in Fig. 8(c). From the phase diagram (Fig. 2), we find that within the scope of our study, the system excited by circularly polarized light does not favor a stable long-range order at M. Nevertheless, we will demonstrate in a subsequent work that via optical modulation, a stable long-range or-der at M and the quasi-long-range order with nontrivial topological features can still be realized on this triangular metallic AFM magnet.  

> IV. DISCUSSIONS AND CONCLUSIONS

We have uncovered various novel magnetic orders in the simple photoexcited triangular metallic magnet, in-cluding the FM state, vortex state, quasi-long-range or-der at K, long-range order at K/2 and K′/2, and dy-namical long-range order at M. We then provide a de-6

In contrast to previ-ous studies, we argue that a weaker DE interaction and a AFM equilibrium state give smaller energy band gap. Thus it is more favorable for the optical field to induce electronic excitations. Meanwhile, the abundant equi-librium magnetic states in the phase diagram of J and 

n space on triangular DM model also indicate that the magnetic configuration is extremely sensitive to the elec-tronic state, which facilitates the observation of these rich electronic and magnetic phases [31]. Low-frequency excitations in the range 0 < ω < 3are investigated as well, but not presented in this pa-per. In A0 − ω phase diagram of this low-ω region, weak FM orders are dominant, with disordered states scattered throughout. To realize all these states, we estimate the requisite electric field strength E0 = A0ω is on the order of 1 ∼ 100 MV/cm. However, the continuous-wave opti-cal intensity achievable in the laboratory currently is just on the order of 10 MV/cm. Besides, field intensities of this magnitude are sufficiently high to induce material degradation. This issue can probably be addressed in two ways. For one thing, periodic terahertz pulse can be employed instead of continuous-wave light in the hope of achieving a similar effect [36]. For another, other C6-invariant lattices with more fragile magnetic structures can be chosen. Both of these aspects have been explored preliminarily in our subsequent work.
