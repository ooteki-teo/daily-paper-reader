# Ladder-like Structural Architecture of Layered Magnetic A2.4 Cr 8Te 14 (A = Rb, Cs) Compounds by Self-flux Synthesis 

# Kai D. Röseler, [a] Felix Eder, [a] Fabian O. von Rohr,* [a] 

The discovery and control of intergrowth structures rep-resent an important avenue for the targeted synthesis of new, more complex structure types. When including mag-netic framework metal atoms, this enhanced complexity can transfer to rich magnetic ground states. Here, we show that the subtle adjustment of the composition of alkali-tellurium fluxes enables the synthesis of a new fam-ily of alkali chromium tellurides, A2.4 Cr 8Te 14 (A = Rb, Cs). Their ladder-like crystal structures integrate the two-dimensional character of delafossite-like ACrTe 2 with the tunnel motifs of hollandite-like AxCr 5Te 8 phases. This re-sults in a previously unobserved unique hybrid framework. Direction-dependent magnetization measurements on ori-ented single crystals reveal distinct magnetic ground states: Rb 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 is antiferromagnetic with TN = 114.5 K, while Cs 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 is ferrimagnetic with TC = 125.0 K. This work underscores the simplicity and effectiveness of flux growth as a design strategy for discovering low-dimensional materials. 

# Introduction 

The search for new functional materials increasingly turns to modular “synthesis-by-design” strategies, in which known structural units are deliberately combined to generate tar-geted properties. [1–3] One structural realization of this prin-ciple is the formation of complex structures of compounds that can be interpreted as intergrowth of segments of sim-pler structures. [4,5] Such intergrowth phases often serve as structural bridges between known phases and provide a ra-tional route toward new, more complex architectures. [6,7] 

For this study, we considered the family of ternary alkali-chromium tellurides. In this system, all known phases up to date follow one of two structure types: The first known structure type is found for the smaller alkali cations 

A = Li, Na, K and can be considered as delafossite-like layered phases with a composition of ACrTe 2. The crystal structure of these delafossite-type phases consists of CrTe 2

sheets separated by layers of intercalated alkali cations. [8,9] 

We have recently established a synthetic approach to obtain large single-crystals of delafossite-type ACrX 2 (X = chalcogen) phases by employing a A/X-self-flux synthesis approach. [10–12] It is worth noting that the deintercalation of these phases by soft-chemical methods has become an central strategy for accessing metastable, magnetic, 

[a] K. D. Röseler, Dr. F. Eder, Prof. Dr. F. O. von Rohr* Department of Quantum Matter Physics, University of Geneva, CH-1211 Geneva, Switzerland 

Cr IV -based vdW materials such as CrTe 2. [13,14] 

The second known structure type for A–Cr–Te phases – exhibited by the heavier alkali metals – follows a com-position of AxCr 5Te 8 (A = K, Rb, Cs; 0.73 ≤ x ≤ 1)and corresponds to a hollandite-like architecture. Here, the CrTe 2 layers are interconnected by Cr 2Te 2 slabs forming a tri-periodic framework perforated by channels occupied by alkali cations. [15,16] The synthesis route reported for these phases either used a solid-state reaction of the elements [15] 

(A = K, Rb, Cs), or a reaction of elemental Cr, Te and either Cs 2CO 3 or CsTe 4 as Cs-sources [16] . By altering the amount of Cs-source used, different under-stoichiometric Cs x Cr 5Te 8

phases with 0.73 < x < 1 have been synthesized. Both struc-ture types contain CrTe 2 layers of edge-sharing CrTe 6 oc-tahedra, but the number of Cr 2Te 2 bridges connecting them controls the dimensionality – from two-dimensional in delafossite-type to three-dimensional in hollandite-like phases. This increase in dimensionality is accompanied by a decreases with alkali content. A structure with alternat-ing Cr 2Te 2 bridges and layers of alkali atoms, however, has not yet been reported and motivated our search for new two-dimensional magnetic materials. In this work, we apply flux synthesis strategies to these alkali-chromium-tellurides, revealing a novel ladder-like hy-brid framework with tunable dimensionality and magnetic ground states – opening a new avenue in single-anion ma-terials design, and bridging a logical gap in the (Rb,Cs)-Cr-Te phase space. Specifically, we obtained large crystals of the previously unreported layered phases Cs 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 

and Rb 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 . These compounds exhibit the desired double-layered ladder-like architecture, which can be in-terpreted as a structural intergrowth phase consisting of the structural elements of both the hollandite- and the delafossite-like structures. In direction-dependent magne-tization experiments on oriented single crystals we find that Rb 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 is antiferromagnetic with TN = 114.5 K, while Cs 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 orders ferrimagnetically at T C = 125 K. 

# Experimental 

## Synthesis of Cs 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 

As a first step, a Cs x Te y precursor was prepared by carefully adding drops of liquid Cs (Alfa Aesar, 99.98%) to crushed Te pieces (Alfa Aesar, 99.999%) in an agate mortar inside an Ar-filled glovebox, which led to the rapid formation of un-specified Cs–Te binary compounds, which were ground into a fine powder. Cr (powder, Alfa Aesar, 99.95%) and the Cs x Te y precursor were then placed in a Canfield alumina crucible set comprising a bottom and top crucible and a frit disc in between [17] . The setup was then sealed in a quartz

## Synthesis of Rb 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 

Single crystals of Rb 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 were synthesized using a Rb/Se self flux starting from a molar Rb:Cr:Te ratio of 3.3:1:8. Rb (Strem, 99.9+%), Cr (powder, Alfa Aesar, 99.95%) and Te (pieces, Sigma Aldrich, 99.999%) were placed in a Canfield crucible inside a glovebox with Ar at-mosphere. The crucible was then sealed in a quartz am-pule under an atmosphere of 300 mbar Ar and placed in a muffle furnace. The temperature program followed by the subsequent centrifugation was identical to the synthesis of Cs 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 .

## Synthesis of CsCr 5Te 8

Single crystals of CsCr 5Te 8 were synthesized using a Cs/Te self-flux from molar Cs:Cr:Te ratios of 3.3:1:8 by the same procedure as Cs 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 . By this, black bar-shaped single crystals were obtained. Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) measurements confirmed an almost phase-pure product with small impurities of remaining Cs 2Te 3 and Cs 2Te 2 flux (see ESI). ## Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) 

Capillary PXRD data of Rb 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 and Cs 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 

were collected on a Bruker D8 Discover diffractometer equipped with a LynxeyeXE detector using Mo-K α radi-ation ( λ = 0.710806 Å) in a 2 θ scan in the range of 2.5–49 °.The beam was shaped by a 6 mm focussing Göbel mirror and size selected by a 0.6 mm slit. Capillary PXRD mea-surements on CsCr 5Te 8 were performed on a Rigaku Smart-Lab diffractometer with Cu-K α radiation ( λ = 1.54187 Å) on a D/teX Ultra 250 detector in the range of 5 °–80 °. For this, CsCr 5Te 8 was diluted with amorphous quartz dust. Calcu-lated patterns were simulated using the Mercury software developed by the Cambridge Crystallographic Data Cen-tre. [18] Full width half maximum (FWHM) values were es-timated to match the experimental pattern best. Patterns of Rb 2Te 3, Cs 2Te 3, and Cs 2Te 2 were generated based on crys-tal structures from the ICSD database [19] : Rb 2Te 3 (90806), Cs 2Te 3 (53244), and Cs 2Te 2 (83351). 

## Single crystal X-ray diffraction (SXRD) 

SXRD experiments were performed under N 2 cooling at 100 K on an Oxford Diffraction Supernova diffractome-ter using Mo K α radiation ( λ = 0.71072 Å). Pre-experiment screenings, data collection, data reduction, and absorption correction were performed using the program suite CrysAl-isPro. [20] The crystal structure was solved with the dual space method in SHELXT. [21] Least squares refinements of F2 were performed using SHELXL [22] .

## Magnetization measurements 

Magnetization versus temperature and magnetization versus magnetic field measurements were carried out in a Physical Property Measurement System (PPMS DynaCool) from Quantum Design equipped with the vibrating sample magnetometer (VSM) option. The measurements were performed in a temperature range of 1.8 – 300 K in the sweep mode at rates of 5 K ·min −1 and 50 Oe ·s−1 in the range of -9 T to 9 T. 

## Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) 

Electron images were obtained from a JEOL JSM-IT800 Scanning electron microscope with an acceleration voltage of 20 kV. Energy dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS) data was collected with an X-Max N 80 detector from Oxford Instruments. Composition measurements are based on 80 measurement points at eight sites on four crystals from two batches each. 

# Results and Discussion 

## Crystal structures of Rb 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 and Cs 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 

Metallic-black crystals of Rb 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 (starting from a flux with molar Rb:Cr:Te ratios of 3.3:1:8) with sizes up to 4 

× 3 × 0.2 mm 3 and Cs 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 (starting from a flux with molar Cs:Cr:Te ratios of 6:1:8; size up to 3 × 1 ×

0.2 mm 3) were obtained from self-flux syntheses followed by hot-centrifugation (Photographs Figure 2). Powder X-ray diffraction (PXRD) identified Rb 2Te 3 or Cs 2Te 3 and Cs 2Te 2, as the only detectable side products, resulting from residual flux material (see ESI). When the Cs-based syn-thesis is performed with a molar 3.3:1:8 ratio of the start-ing elements instead, crystals of the hollandite-like phase CsCr 5Te 8 are obtained; these were characterized by PXRD and thanks to increased crystal size subjected to anisotropic magnetic measurements for the first time. This demon-strates that in the (Rb,Cs)–Cr–Te system, the flux compo-sition operates as a control parameter on phase formation. In con-trast, in AxCr 5Te 8 phases, these CrTe 2 layers are instead interconnected by alternating Cr 2Te 2 bridges and A cations. Through this, a tri-periodic Cr–Te framework permeated by channels along [010] is formed. In A2.4 Cr 8Te 14 , both of these two possibilities of linking CrTe 2 layers are real-ized alternately. Pairs of CrTe 2 layers are interconnected by Cr 2Te 2 bridges leading to a ladder-shaped Cr 8Te 14 double-layer. Like their counterparts within the CrTe 2 layers, the CrTe 6 coordination polyhedra of the two bridging Cr atoms are connected to each other by edge-sharing. However, the contact between CrTe 2 layers and Cr 2Te 2 bridges is fa-cilitated by plane-sharing instead, thus resulting in much shorter Cr—Cr distances at these points. Instead of 3.8– 4.0 Å, the Cr—Cr contacts are as close as 3.078(15) and 3.087(15) Å (Cs-phase) or 3.061(10) and 3.065(10) Å (Rb-phase). The Cr 2Te 2 bridge further influence the Cr—Cr distances within the CrTe 2 layers: contacts along <010 > are near 3.92 Å, while those along <130 > alternate between shorter (3.8–3.9 Å) and longer ( ≈ 4.0 Å) separations. This distance disproportionation reflects the influence of the interconnect-ing Cr 2Te 2 bridges bound by plane-sharing, as the Cr sites with the most distorted in-plane Cr—Cr contacts are those directly involved in the cross-connections. The Cr 8Te 14 ladders are isolated from each other by a layer of A cations. While the A sites located inside the hollandite-type tunnels are fully occupied, as they are in CsCr 5Te 8 as well, those forming the separating layer are occupationally disordered with site occupation factors close to 0.5. This leads to the non-integer sum formula of 

A2.4 Cr 8Te 14 , as a full occupation of all A sites would instead lead to a sum formula of A4Cr 8Te 14 . The A content is very similar in the presented SXRD refinements of Rb 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 

and Cs 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 . The A2.4 Cr 8Te 14 stoichiometry of both phases was later confirmed by EDS measurements discussed in detail below. Under-stoichiometry of the A cations is frequently observed in delafossite-like phases, including the cathode materials Na x CoO 2 and Li x CoO 2 [23,24] , or the chromium based phases K 0.6–0.8 CrSe 2 [25,26] , K 0.87 CrSe 2 [11] 

and K 1– x CrSe 2 [27] , as well as in hollandite-like Ax Cr 5Te 8

(A = Rb, Cs; 0.619 < x < 1) [16] . In all cases, the removal of A cations leads to a gradual increase of the formal Cr oxidation number from pure Cr III to a mixed Cr III /Cr IV 

valence state. These interlayer A sites are the only part of the crystal structure that breaks C 2/ m symmetry, which is followed by the Cr 8Te 14 ladders. The consequences of this on the refinement are discussed in detail in the ESI. 

## Differences between Cs 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 and Rb 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 

At first glance, the unit cell metrics and crystal structures of Cs 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 and Rb 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 appear very similar. The 

a and b lattice parameters, which are mostly defined by the dimensions of the Cr 8Te 14 ladders, are almost identical. The larger value of c for Cs 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 can be attributed to the higher ionic radius of Cs compared to Rb. However, the most striking difference concerns the ori-entation of the Cr 2Te 2 bridges relative to c. While in Cs 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 , they are located parallel to c, in Rb 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 , they are tilted in the other direction rela-tive to c* . This indicates that the stacking of the whole Cr 8Te 14 layers is different for the two phases (see Figure 3(b,c)). This difference cannot be resolved by a different setting of the unit cell. When progressing along the tilted direction of the Cr 2Te 2 bridges (dashed arrows in Figure 3(b,c)), the Cr atoms alternate pairwise between y = 0 and 

y = 1/2 in Cs 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 , whereas in Rb 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 this al-ternation follows a repeating sequence of y = 0, 1/2, 1/2, 0 along the bridge direction. This clear difference in the stacking arrangement of the double-layers underlines the presence of two different structure types in Cs 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 ,and Rb 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 .A similar distinction between two closely related struc-ture types is known for the pseudo-hollandite ACr 5Te 8

## Microstructure and elemental composition 

EDS analysis was performed on Cs 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 and Rb 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 crystals to verify the elemental composi-tion, especially the alkali content determined by the SXRD experiments. EDS measurements based on 80 measure-ment points at eight sites on four crystals from two batches of search found the elemental composition to be Cs 2.9(4) Cr 8Te 14.6(8) , which shows that the Cs and Te con-tents are in very good agreement with our SXRD experi-ment. The elementary composition of Rb 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 based on EDS was found to be Rb 2.56(20) Cr 8Te 13.7(3) which fits the values from SXRD within one standard deviation. Both the alkali metal, as well as the Te contents are slightly ele-vated in comparison with the refined structural model from SXRD, which might be caused by some residual flux stuck to the uncleaved sample surface. According to EDS analyses, Cs 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 appears to contain more excess flux, which is in line with traces of Cs 2Te 2 observed in PXRD, which is expected to be solid at the centrifugation temperature. [28] 

It should be noted that the chemical composition, especially for Rb 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 , was found not to vary much when com-paring different spots on the same crystal, different crystals as well as crystals from different batches. This suggests homogeneity of the composition within the crystals and the batches as well as the reproducibility of the synthesis. Maps of the elemental compositions with two different magnifica-tions further underlining the homogeneity of the crystals are depicted in the ESI.The chemical composition of flux-grown CsCr 5Te 8 was determined as Cs 1.01(11) Cr 5Te 8.1(4) ,which exactly fits the expected stoichiometry and does not indicate any under-occupation of the Cs sites in the chan-nels. A list of the individual elemental compositions within the batches, crystals, and sites for all three compounds is given in the ESI. Using scanning electron microscopy, we further studied the microstructure of Cs 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 and Rb 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 , the results of which are depicted in Figure 2(b,c). Both com-pounds form overall plate-shaped crystals that show large areas of flat surfaces. Large magnifications show steps sug-gesting stacks of multilayered, stacked crystal slabs, which is a common microstructural feature of 2D-materials. On top of the surface, smaller on-grown crystals can be ob-served, which were identified as remaining flux AxTe y with no Cr-content. The layered nature of the crystal structure is further highlighted by stair-like features resulting from the increasing number of multi-layers on top of each other. 

## Magnetic properties 

Given the uncommon isolated ladder-like architecture of these compounds, their magnetic behavior is of particular interest, and we have studied it using direction-dependent magnetization on oriented single crystals. This is es-pecially relevant in light of the rich and tunable mag-netism known in structurally related intercalated chromium dichalcogenides, where small variations in size and amount of alkali cations lead to diverse magnetic ground states. Analogously, the closely related ladder-like telluride frame-works of Rb 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 and Cs 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 provide an ex-cellent platform to explore the interplay between crystal structure and magnetism. As shown in Figure 4, the mag-netic moment reveals that the two compounds, despite their structural similarity, order magnetically in different ways: Rb 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 orders antiferromagnetically at TN = 114.5 K, whereas Cs 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 exhibits ferrimagnetic ordering at 

TC = 125.0 K. Temperature-dependent measurements of the magnetic mo-ment of Cs 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 (Figure 4 (a)) show a transition to a ferromagnetic state below TC = 125.0 K. The ferromagnetic transition temperature can be estimated based on the max-ima of the first derivative dm dT corresponding to the inflection point in the m(T ) curves at TC = 125.0 K. The field-dependent magnetization measurements of Cs 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 are presented in Figure 4(b) for T = 1.8 K, and 150 K, with the external magnetic field parallel and perpendicular to c* respectively. Only a small hysteresis loop is visible at T = 1.8 K. For both crystal orientations in the external field, H ∥ c* = 9 T and H ⊥ c* = 9 T the magnetic moment at 1.8 K saturates at about m = 1.24 

μB /Cr. Since the magnetic moment saturates much faster, when the external field is applied parallel to the layer plane (H ⊥ c* ), the magnetic easy-axis and direction of the mag-netic moments are likely to lie within the layer plane as well. The theoretical magnetic moment can be estimated using the spin-only formula m = g · S · μB (assuming no spin-orbit coupling) with S being the total spin and g be-ing the Lande factor, which can be approximated as being 2. Since the formal oxidation number of Cr in A2.4 Cr 8Te 14 

amounts to +3.2, we estimate the magnetic moment from a mixture of the adjacent +3 and +4 oxidation states. For Cr 4+ and Cr 3+ the expected moments are 2 μB and 3 μB

respectively, leading to a theoretically expected moment of 2.8 μB/Cr atom, which is significantly higher than the ob-served saturation moment. This difference can be ratio-nalized by ferrimagnetic ordering within the Cr 8Te 14 lad-ders, instead of a pure ferromagnetic ground state. The two types of CrTe 6 octahedra-linking by edge- and face-sharing and the resulting two groups of nearest-neighbor Cr—Cr-distances provide the structural basis for such a magnetic or-dering. According to the Goodenough–Kanamori–Anderson rules, [29–33] the edge–sharing CrTe 6 octahedra with Cr–Te– Cr angles close to 90 ◦ favor ferromagnetic superexchange, while the Cr III —Cr III contacts evoke antiferromagnetic di-rect exchange; the latter contribution becomes increasingly relevant as the Cr—Cr distance decreases. Within the CrTe 2 layers, the large Cr—Cr distances of 3.8–4.0 Å make ferromagnetic interactions favorable. In contrast, the short Cr—Cr contacts (< 3.1 Å) that connect the Cr 2Te 2 slabs to the CrTe 2 layers are very likely antiferromagnetically cou-pled. Assuming two spin orientations along the easy axis, this geometry would couple the two CrTe 2 layers ferromag-netically while the bridging Cr 2Te 2 units adopt the opposite spin orientation. With six Cr atoms per formula unit in the CrTe 2 layers and two in the bridges, the net moment is re-duced by a factor of 6−28 = 12 , which is consistent with the reduced saturation magnetization observed in Cs 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 .This hypothesis is underpinned by the magnetic satura-tion moments of CsCr 5Te 8 observed in the magnetic field-dependent measurements in Figure 4(d). The saturation moment for an applied magnetic field H ⊥ c* (along the easy magnetization axis) corresponds to slightly above 0.5 μB . # Conclusion 

We have extended the family of alkali chromium tel-lurides by discovering two phases with new structure types, 

A2.4 Cr 8Te 14 (A = Rb, Cs). These compounds were ob-tained as millimeter-sized single crystals via A/Te self-flux syntheses, highlighting the potential of flux growth not only for producing high–quality crystals but also as a materials discovery tool. Their crystal structures consist of a ladder-like hybrid framework that integrates motifs from both delafossite-like 

ACrTe 2 and hollandite-like ACr 5Te 8 phases, representing a flux-guided intergrowth of 2D and 3D endmembers, and thus highlighting the possibility of a new structure series from these building blocks. Our large single crystals enabled us to perform anisotropic magnetic measurements of these phases. De-spite their close structural relationship, the two com-pounds exhibit distinctively different magnetic ground states: Rb 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 undergoes antiferromagnetic ordering below TN = 114.5 K with field-induced metamagnetic tran-sition at higher fields, while Cs 2.4 Cr 8Te 14 exhibits a ferri-magnetic ground state with TC = 125.0 K and reduced sat-uration magnetization. These findings underscore the sensi-tivity of magnetic order to subtle structural and electronic differences within the ladder framework. The competition between ferromagnetic and antiferromagnetic interactions at different Cr—Cr distances drives the observed long-range magnetic orders. By extending intergrowth concepts to magnetic chromium-chalchogenides through simple flux synthesis control, this study opens new directions for targeted synthesis in magnetic 2D quantum materials research. The layered architecture of A2.4 Cr 8Te 14 provides a promising foundation for further structural series of intergrown alkali-chalchogenide ternaries, as well as for manipulation by deintercalation, metathesis, or intercalation chemistry, offering a pathway to tune their dimensionality and elec-tronic states. Such chemically engineered derivatives may unlock access to new families of two-dimensional spintronic or quantum materials.
